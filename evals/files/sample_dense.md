## Pathophysiology of Hemorrhagic Shock

Hemorrhagic shock represents a critical state of inadequate tissue perfusion resulting from acute blood loss. The pathophysiology involves a complex cascade of compensatory mechanisms that ultimately fail when blood loss exceeds the body's ability to compensate. Initial responses include sympathetic activation with tachycardia and peripheral vasoconstriction, followed by hormonal responses including ACTH, ADH, and renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system activation. As hemorrhage progresses beyond Class II (15-30% blood volume loss), compensatory mechanisms begin to fail, leading to progressive hypotension, altered mental status, and end-organ dysfunction. The cellular response involves a shift from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism, leading to lactic acidosis, cellular swelling, and ultimately cell death through both necrotic and apoptotic pathways. Mitochondrial dysfunction plays a central role in the irreversibility of shock, as damaged mitochondria release damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) that trigger systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS). This inflammatory cascade can progress to multiple organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS) even after hemorrhage control is achieved, a phenomenon known as the "second hit" theory of trauma.

- Initial compensatory response is sympathetic nervous system activation
- Tachycardia occurs first as the heart rate increases to maintain cardiac output
- Peripheral vasoconstriction redirects blood to vital organs
- ACTH release stimulates cortisol production for stress response
- ADH promotes water retention to maintain intravascular volume
- Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system activates for sodium and water retention
- Cellular shift to anaerobic metabolism produces lactic acid
- Mitochondrial dysfunction leads to DAMP release
- SIRS cascade can progress to MODS
- "Second hit" phenomenon occurs even after hemorrhage control

## Fluid Resuscitation Strategies

The management of hemorrhagic shock has evolved significantly over the past two decades with the recognition that aggressive crystalloid resuscitation can worsen outcomes through dilutional coagulopathy, hypothermia, and abdominal compartment syndrome. The damage control resuscitation paradigm emphasizes permissive hypotension, hemostatic resuscitation with balanced blood product ratios, and early surgical hemorrhage control. The PROPPR trial (2015) established the optimal plasma:platelet:RBC ratio as 1:1:1, demonstrating reduced 24-hour mortality compared to 1:1:2 ratios. Whole blood resuscitation has gained renewed interest based on military experience and civilian trials showing potential benefits in simplification and hemostatic efficacy. Tranexamic acid administration within 3 hours of injury has been shown to reduce mortality in the CRASH-2 trial, though the MATTERs study suggested even greater benefit in military settings. Current guidelines from both EAST and the European Trauma Guidelines recommend early TXA administration. Viscoelastic hemostatic assays (TEG/ROTEM) enable goal-directed transfusion therapy, reducing blood product waste and improving outcomes through personalized coagulation management. The role of calcium supplementation during massive transfusion has been increasingly recognized, with ionized calcium levels below 1.0 mmol/L associated with increased mortality.

- Aggressive crystalloid resuscitation worsens outcomes
- Dilutional coagulopathy is a major concern
- Hypothermia from cold fluids compounds the problem
- Abdominal compartment syndrome risk increases with large volume crystalloid
- PROPPR trial: 1:1:1 ratio superior to 1:1:2
- Whole blood gaining renewed interest
- TXA within 3 hours reduces mortality (CRASH-2)
- MATTERs study showed TXA benefit in military
- EAST guidelines recommend early TXA
- European Trauma Guidelines also recommend TXA
- TEG/ROTEM enables goal-directed therapy
- Calcium supplementation increasingly important

## Tourniquet Application

Tourniquets are important tools for hemorrhage control in extremity bleeding.

| Device      | Application Time | Efficacy | Evidence Level |
| ----------- | ---------------- | -------- | -------------- |
| CAT Gen 7   | 30-60 seconds    | 97%      | High           |
| SOF-TT Wide | 30-60 seconds    | 95%      | Moderate       |
| RATS        | 15-30 seconds    | 89%      | Low            |
| Improvised  | Variable         | 60-70%   | Very Low       |

> **Bottom Line:** CAT Gen 7 is the recommended first-line tourniquet.

## Opening Case: Mr. Johnson

A 45-year-old male presents to the trauma bay after a high-speed motorcycle collision. He was found at the scene with obvious bilateral lower extremity deformities and active hemorrhage from a right thigh wound. Field tourniquets were applied by paramedics. On arrival, he is tachycardic at 130, hypotensive at 82/50, and has a GCS of 14 (E4V4M6).

- HR 130, BP 82/50, RR 28, SpO2 94% on NRB
- GCS 14 (E4V4M6)
- Bilateral femur fractures
- Right thigh laceration with active hemorrhage
- FAST positive in Morison's pouch

## Management Algorithm

This slide describes the management approach for hemorrhagic shock patients presenting to the emergency department. The algorithm involves initial assessment, resuscitation, and definitive care. First, establish two large-bore IVs and begin massive transfusion protocol. Second, perform a FAST exam to identify free fluid. Third, if FAST positive and hemodynamically unstable, proceed to the operating room for damage control surgery. Fourth, if FAST negative but patient remains unstable, consider other sources of hemorrhage including pelvic fractures, long bone fractures, and retroperitoneal hemorrhage. The management must be tailored to the individual patient's response to resuscitation and the specific pattern of injuries identified.

- Establish two large-bore IVs (14-16 gauge)
- Activate massive transfusion protocol immediately
- Perform bedside FAST examination
- Type and crossmatch for 6 units packed RBCs
- Administer tranexamic acid 1g IV bolus within 3 hours
- Consider permissive hypotension (MAP 50-60 mmHg)
- If FAST positive + unstable → OR for damage control surgery
- If FAST negative + unstable → CT angiography or angioembolization
- Reassess response to initial resuscitation every 15 minutes
