# MCQ Case-Pairs Template

## Purpose

Generate high-quality multiple choice questions that test **clinical reasoning and evidence application**, not just factual recall. Each question is grounded in a realistic clinical scenario and requires synthesizing evidence to make clinical decisions.

## How to Use This Template

1. **Map to Learning Objectives**: Each MCQ must align with at least one LO from your evidence synthesis
2. **Target Higher Bloom's Levels**: Aim for Application (70%), Analysis (20%), Evaluation (10%). Avoid pure Knowledge/Comprehension questions.
3. **Use Real Evidence**: Every explanation must cite actual studies (PMID) or guidelines (Society, Year, Grade)
4. **Create Plausible Distractors**: Wrong answers should reflect common misconceptions, outdated practices, or reasonable but suboptimal choices
5. **Progressive Difficulty**: Start with foundational cases, build to complex scenarios

## Bloom's Taxonomy Quick Reference

- **Knowledge/Comprehension** (AVOID): "What is the definition of...?"
- **Application** (TARGET 70%): "Given this patient, what is the next best step?"
- **Analysis** (TARGET 20%): "Which study design best addresses this question?"
- **Evaluation** (TARGET 10%): "In a patient with contraindications to standard therapy, what is the best alternative?"

## MCQ Format Standard

Every question follows this exact structure:

```
### Case [N]: [Brief descriptor]

**Clinical Scenario:**
[2-4 sentence patient presentation with relevant history, vitals, labs]

**Question:**
[Single best answer question testing evidence application]

A. [Distractor — common misconception, cite why wrong]
B. [Distractor — outdated practice, cite guideline]
C. [Correct answer]
D. [Distractor — plausible but suboptimal, cite evidence]

**Correct Answer:** C

**Explanation:**
[2-3 sentences explaining why C is correct with citation]
- Evidence: [Author et al., Journal Year; PMID]
- Guideline: [Society recommendation, Grade of evidence]

**Why other answers are wrong:**
- A: [Brief explanation with citation]
- B: [Brief explanation with citation]
- D: [Brief explanation with citation]

**Learning Objective:** LO-[N]
**Bloom's Level:** [Application/Analysis/Evaluation]
**Difficulty:** [Easy/Medium/Hard]
```

## Template Sections

### 1. Foundational Cases (Easy, 3-4 questions)

**Purpose**: Test basic pathophysiology, initial assessment, and first-line management
**Bloom's Level**: Application
**Target Audience**: Medical students, junior residents

#### Case Template (Foundational)

```
### Case F1: [Patient presentation with classic findings]

**Clinical Scenario:**
A [age] [gender] presents to [setting] with [chief complaint]. [Relevant history]. Vital signs: BP [value], HR [value], RR [value], Temp [value], SpO2 [value]. Physical exam shows [key finding]. Labs: [1-2 critical values].

**Question:**
What is the most appropriate next step in management?

A. [Premature intervention before diagnosis]
B. [Outdated/disproven intervention]
C. [Correct: evidence-based first-line approach]
D. [Plausible but suboptimal alternative]

**Correct Answer:** C

**Explanation:**
[Why C is correct based on current guidelines/evidence]
- Evidence: [Citation with PMID]
- Guideline: [Society, Year, Grade]

**Why other answers are wrong:**
- A: [Explain premature intervention risk, cite guideline]
- B: [Cite study showing harm or lack of benefit, PMID]
- D: [Explain why suboptimal despite being reasonable]

**Learning Objective:** LO-[N]
**Bloom's Level:** Application
**Difficulty:** Easy
```

**Repeat this template 3-4 times, varying:**

- Clinical settings (ED, ICU, outpatient)
- Patient demographics (age, comorbidities)
- Presentation acuity (acute vs chronic)

### 2. Evidence Application Cases (Medium, 4-5 questions)

**Purpose**: Test knowledge of current guidelines, interpretation of trial data (NNT, CI, ARR)
**Bloom's Level**: Analysis
**Target Audience**: Senior residents, fellows

#### Case Template (Evidence Application)

```
### Case E1: [Scenario requiring guideline knowledge or trial interpretation]

**Clinical Scenario:**
A [age] [gender] with [comorbidities] presents with [condition]. [Relevant diagnostic workup]. You consider [intervention] based on [trial name/guideline].

**Question:**
Based on the best available evidence, which statement is most accurate regarding [intervention]?

A. [Misinterpretation of trial results — e.g., confusing RRR with ARR]
B. [Correct interpretation with appropriate caveats]
C. [Overgeneralization beyond trial population]
D. [Outdated recommendation superseded by newer evidence]

**Correct Answer:** B

**Explanation:**
[Explain correct interpretation of evidence, including numerical data]
- Evidence: [Trial name, Author et al., Journal Year; PMID]
  - Primary outcome: [specific result with CI]
  - NNT: [value] for [outcome]
  - Applicability: [population, setting]
- Guideline: [Society, Year, Grade]

**Why other answers are wrong:**
- A: [Explain statistical misinterpretation, cite correct calculation]
- C: [Explain trial exclusion criteria, cite PMID]
- D: [Cite newer evidence superseding, PMID]

**Learning Objective:** LO-[N]
**Bloom's Level:** Analysis
**Difficulty:** Medium
```

**Repeat this template 4-5 times, testing:**

- Interpretation of NNT/NNH
- Understanding confidence intervals and p-values
- Applying trial inclusion/exclusion criteria to real patients
- Recognizing when guidelines conflict
- Weighing benefits vs harms (e.g., anticoagulation decisions)

### 3. Complex Clinical Scenarios (Hard, 3-4 questions)

**Purpose**: Multi-step reasoning, competing priorities, special populations
**Bloom's Level**: Evaluation/Synthesis
**Target Audience**: Fellows, attendings

#### Case Template (Complex)

```
### Case C1: [Multi-problem patient with competing priorities]

**Clinical Scenario:**
A [age] [gender] with [multiple comorbidities] presents with [primary problem]. [Additional complicating factor]. Medications include [list with potential interactions]. Vital signs: [values]. Labs: [multiple abnormal values]. Imaging: [key finding].

**Question:**
Given this patient's multiple medical issues, what is the most appropriate management approach?

A. [Addresses primary problem but ignores critical contraindication]
B. [Conservative approach that delays necessary intervention]
C. [Correct: balances competing risks, evidence-based]
D. [Aggressive approach with excessive risk in this population]

**Correct Answer:** C

**Explanation:**
[Explain risk-benefit analysis specific to this patient's profile]
- Evidence for primary intervention: [Citation, PMID]
- Evidence for modification in special population: [Citation, PMID]
- Risk stratification: [Tool/score if applicable]
- Guideline: [Society, Year, Grade with specific population note]

**Why other answers are wrong:**
- A: [Cite contraindication/interaction, PMID or drug reference]
- B: [Cite evidence for timely intervention, PMID]
- D: [Cite increased harm in this population, PMID]

**Learning Objective:** LO-[N]
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluation
**Difficulty:** Hard
```

**Repeat this template 3-4 times, varying:**

- Renal/hepatic impairment requiring dose adjustment
- Pregnancy/lactation considerations
- Polypharmacy and drug interactions
- Geriatric patients with frailty
- Pediatric dosing and developmental considerations
- Patients declining standard therapy (religious, personal preference)

### 4. Controversy Cases (Hard, 2-3 questions)

**Purpose**: Areas where evidence is conflicting or guidelines disagree
**Bloom's Level**: Evaluation
**Target Audience**: Fellows, attendings preparing for boards

#### Case Template (Controversy)

```
### Case V1: [Scenario where multiple approaches are defensible]

**Clinical Scenario:**
A [age] [gender] with [condition] requires [decision]. Guideline A (Society 1, Year) recommends [approach X], citing [trial/evidence]. Guideline B (Society 2, Year) recommends [approach Y], citing [different evidence]. [Patient-specific factors that might favor one approach].

**Question:**
Which factor most strongly supports choosing [approach X] over [approach Y] in this patient?

A. [Factor that doesn't actually differentiate the approaches]
B. [Factor favoring Y, not X — tests careful reading]
C. [Correct: evidence-based factor favoring X in this context]
D. [Factor that is irrelevant to both approaches]

**Correct Answer:** C

**Explanation:**
[Explain why C tips the balance toward approach X in this specific context]
- Evidence for X in this subgroup: [Citation, PMID]
- Evidence for Y (acknowledging strength): [Citation, PMID]
- Key differentiating factor: [specific patient characteristic or outcome priority]
- Guideline context: [Explain why guidelines differ]

**Why other answers are wrong:**
- A: [Explain why this factor doesn't differentiate, cite both guidelines]
- B: [This actually favors Y, cite evidence]
- D: [Explain irrelevance, cite evidence]

**Learning Objective:** LO-[N]
**Bloom's Level:** Evaluation
**Difficulty:** Hard
```

**Repeat this template 2-3 times for areas such as:**

- Anticoagulation vs antiplatelet in specific scenarios
- Antibiotic duration debates (e.g., CAP 5d vs 7d vs clinical stability criteria)
- Imaging decisions where guidelines differ (e.g., PE workup)
- Threshold debates (e.g., transfusion triggers)
- Screening controversies (e.g., PSA, mammography age cutoffs)

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing your MCQ set, verify:

### Structure

- [ ] Each question has exactly 4 options (A-D)
- [ ] Only ONE unambiguously correct answer per question
- [ ] All distractors are plausible (no obvious throwaway options)
- [ ] Clinical scenarios include specific vitals/labs (not vague)
- [ ] Questions are independent (answering Q1 doesn't give away Q2)

### Evidence Rigor

- [ ] Every explanation includes a citation (PMID or guideline)
- [ ] All citations are real and accurate (verify before publication)
- [ ] Numerical data (NNT, CI, p-values) are correct
- [ ] Guidelines cited with year and grade of recommendation
- [ ] No outdated evidence (check publication dates)

### Educational Value

- [ ] Questions test APPLICATION/ANALYSIS, not just RECALL
- [ ] No "all of the above" or "none of the above" options
- [ ] No "always" or "never" wording (except when clinically accurate)
- [ ] Difficulty progression: Easy → Medium → Hard
- [ ] Each Learning Objective covered by at least 2 questions
- [ ] Mix of settings (ED, ICU, floor, outpatient)
- [ ] Diverse patient demographics (age, gender, comorbidities)

### Distractors

- [ ] Each distractor has a cited reason why it's wrong
- [ ] Distractors reflect real clinical misconceptions
- [ ] At least one distractor is "outdated practice"
- [ ] At least one distractor is "plausible but suboptimal"
- [ ] No distractors that are dangerous/absurd

## Example: Completed MCQ

### Case E2: Antibiotic Duration in CAP

**Clinical Scenario:**
A 58-year-old woman with type 2 diabetes presents to the ED with fever (38.9°C), productive cough, and dyspnea. Vital signs: BP 118/72, HR 98, RR 22, SpO2 92% on room air. CXR shows right lower lobe consolidation. Labs: WBC 14.2, procalcitonin 0.8 ng/mL. She is admitted for community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) and started on ceftriaxone and azithromycin. By hospital day 3, she is afebrile with improved oxygen saturation.

**Question:**
Based on current evidence, what is the most appropriate antibiotic duration for this patient?

A. Continue antibiotics for 10 days total (7 more days)
B. Continue antibiotics until procalcitonin normalizes
C. Discontinue antibiotics if clinically stable (total 5 days)
D. Continue antibiotics for 14 days given diabetes

**Correct Answer:** C

**Explanation:**
Multiple RCTs have demonstrated non-inferiority of 5-day antibiotic courses for CAP when patients achieve clinical stability (afebrile, improved respiratory symptoms, stable vitals). This approach reduces antibiotic exposure without increasing treatment failure.

- Evidence: Uranga et al., JAMA Intern Med 2016; PMID 27455166 (5d vs 10d, non-inferior)
- Evidence: Dimopoulos et al., Am J Respir Crit Care Med 2013; PMID 23098989 (clinical stability criteria)
- Guideline: IDSA/ATS CAP Guidelines 2019 (minimum 5 days if clinically stable, Grade B)

**Why other answers are wrong:**

- A: Traditional 10-14 day courses are no longer recommended for uncomplicated CAP. Shorter courses reduce antibiotic resistance and C. difficile risk (Vaughn et al., Clin Infect Dis 2014; PMID 24785230)
- B: Procalcitonin-guided therapy is used in some European protocols but not standard in US guidelines. Her initial procalcitonin (0.8) supports bacterial infection, but repeat testing is not required if clinically stable (Schuetz et al., Cochrane 2017; PMID 28718395)
- D: Diabetes alone does not require prolonged therapy in uncomplicated CAP. Duration is based on clinical stability, not comorbidities (IDSA/ATS 2019)

**Learning Objective:** LO-3 (Apply evidence-based antibiotic stewardship principles)
**Bloom's Level:** Application
**Difficulty:** Medium

## Notes for Question Writers

1. **Avoid these common pitfalls:**
   - Questions that can be answered without reading the scenario
   - Distractors that are medically dangerous (no one would choose)
   - "Trick questions" that hinge on obscure wording
   - Multiple correct answers (if you must, ask "MOST appropriate")
   - Negative phrasing ("Which is NOT...") — confusing and low-yield

2. **Strong distractor strategies:**
   - Common dosing errors (e.g., wrong unit conversion)
   - Interventions appropriate for different condition (e.g., PE vs MI workup)
   - Correct intervention but wrong timing (e.g., thrombolytics after time window)
   - Older guidelines superseded by newer evidence

3. **Make scenarios realistic:**
   - Use actual vital sign ranges for the condition
   - Include red herrings (normal findings that don't change management)
   - Add time pressure for ED/ICU scenarios
   - Specify resource limitations for practical questions

4. **Citation best practices:**
   - PMID for all primary research articles
   - Society + Year + Grade for guideline recommendations
   - Include trial acronym if widely known (GUSTO, CREST, SPRINT)
   - For meta-analyses, cite Cochrane PMID if available

## Template Variables to Customize

When generating MCQs from evidence synthesis, replace these placeholders:

- `[age]` — Vary across lifespan (25, 58, 72, 89)
- `[gender]` — Balance male/female/non-binary representation
- `[comorbidities]` — DM, CKD, COPD, CAD, cirrhosis, pregnancy
- `[setting]` — ED, ICU, medical floor, outpatient clinic
- `[vitals]` — Use realistic ranges for condition severity
- `[labs]` — Include 2-3 abnormal values max (avoid "laundry list")
- `[LO-N]` — Map to specific learning objectives from synthesis
- `[Author et al.]` — Replace with actual study authors
- `[Journal Year]` — Use publication year
- `[PMID]` — Insert actual PubMed ID
- `[Society]` — IDSA, AHA, ACC, ACOG, etc.
- `[Grade]` — A/B/C or I/IIa/IIb/III depending on system

## Quality Assurance Process

Before releasing MCQ set:

1. **Peer Review**: Have 2 content experts review for accuracy
2. **Citation Check**: Verify every PMID and guideline reference
3. **Pilot Testing**: Trial with target audience, collect item analysis data
4. **Revision**: Drop questions with <30% or >90% correct rate
5. **Finalize**: Lock answer key, create scoring rubric
