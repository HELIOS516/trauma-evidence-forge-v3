# NBME Surgery Shelf Exam: Trauma Surgery Deep Dive

> Comprehensive analysis of trauma-specific topics tested on the NBME Surgery Subject Exam.
> Generated 2026-03-23 via multi-source web research.
> Scope: **Trauma surgery ONLY** — excludes EGS (appendicitis, cholecystitis, SBO, pancreatitis, etc.) and perioperative medicine.

---

## 1. How Much of the Shelf is Trauma?

### NBME Official System Mapping (Trauma Spans Multiple Systems)

The NBME does NOT have a standalone "Trauma" category. Trauma questions are distributed across organ systems:

| System | % of Exam | Trauma Content Within |
|--------|-----------|----------------------|
| Cardiovascular | 10-15% | Hemorrhagic shock, cardiac tamponade, aortic injury |
| Respiratory | 8-12% | Pneumothorax, hemothorax, flail chest, pulmonary contusion |
| Nervous System | 5-10% | TBI, epidural/subdural, spinal cord injury, herniation |
| Musculoskeletal | 3-7% | Pelvic fractures, compartment syndrome, open fractures |
| Skin & Subcutaneous | 1-5% | Burns, escharotomy, wound management |
| Multisystem | 5-10% | ATLS, primary survey, damage control, shock classification |
| Blood & Lymphoreticular | 5-10% | Massive transfusion, coagulopathy, TXA |

### Prep Company Consensus Estimate

| Domain | % of Exam | Source |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Trauma & Critical Care** | **20-25%** | ResidencyAdvisor, MedBoardTutors, multiple prep companies |

This means **~22-28 questions out of 110** are trauma-related.

### Predictive Power

Trauma/Burns quiz scores had the **highest beta-coefficient (1.57, p<.001)** for predicting NBME shelf performance in a study of 156 students (PMC7391907). No other surgical domain predicted shelf scores as strongly.

### Setting Distribution Confirms ED/Trauma Weight

- Emergency Department: **25-35%** of questions
- Many of these ED questions are trauma scenarios

## 2. ATLS Framework & Primary Survey

**This is the single highest-yield trauma topic.** Almost every trauma question on the shelf tests whether you follow ABCDE before diagnostics.

### A — Airway (with C-Spine Protection)
- **Definitive airway** = cuffed tube in trachea (ETT or surgical airway)
- **Indications for intubation:** GCS ≤8, inability to maintain airway, severe facial/neck trauma, expanding neck hematoma
- **Surgical airway (cricothyrotomy):** When oral/nasal intubation fails; contraindicated in children <12 (use needle cricothyrotomy)
- **C-spine immobilization** maintained until cleared

### B — Breathing
- **6 immediately life-threatening injuries** (diagnose clinically, treat before imaging):
  1. Tension pneumothorax
  2. Open pneumothorax (sucking chest wound)
  3. Massive hemothorax
  4. Flail chest with pulmonary contusion
  5. Cardiac tamponade
  6. Airway obstruction
- **Needle decompression:** 2nd intercostal space midclavicular OR 5th ICS anterior axillary line
- **Finger thoracostomy** is definitive decompression (needle fails in obese/muscular patients)

### C — Circulation (Hemorrhage Control)
- **Direct pressure** first for external hemorrhage
- **Two large-bore IVs** (14-16 gauge) + isotonic crystalloid (warm)
- **Assess shock class** (see Section 3)
- **FAST exam** for hemoperitoneum
- **Massive transfusion protocol** activation criteria

### D — Disability (Neurologic)
- **GCS** (see Section 6)
- **Pupil examination** — asymmetry suggests herniation
- **Lateralizing signs** — hemiparesis, posturing

### E — Exposure
- **Log-roll** for posterior exam
- **Prevent hypothermia** — warm blankets, warm fluids, warm room

### KEY SHELF PRINCIPLE
> **Stabilize before you scan.** Any shelf question offering CT as an answer choice in an unstable patient is a distractor. The answer is almost always resuscitation, decompression, or operative intervention first.

## 3. Hemorrhagic Shock

### ATLS Classification

| Class | Blood Loss | HR | BP | RR | Mental Status | Fluid Replacement |
|-------|-----------|------|------|------|--------------|-------------------|
| I | <15% (<750 mL) | <100 | Normal | 14-20 | Slightly anxious | Crystalloid |
| II | 15-30% (750-1500) | 100-120 | Normal | 20-30 | Mildly anxious | Crystalloid |
| III | 30-40% (1500-2000) | >120 | Decreased | 30-40 | Confused | Crystalloid + blood |
| IV | >40% (>2000) | >140 | Very low | >35 | Obtunded | Crystalloid + blood + MTP |

### High-Yield Shelf Points
- **Class III/IV = immediate life threat** → blood products, not just crystalloid
- **Young patients compensate** until sudden decompensation — normal BP does NOT rule out significant hemorrhage
- **Shock Index (HR/SBP):** >1.0 suggests significant hemorrhage; >1.4 predicts need for massive transfusion
- **Base deficit and lactate** are better markers of shock severity than vital signs alone
- **Up to 30% blood loss** can occur before BP drops

### Damage Control Resuscitation (3 Pillars)
1. **Permissive hypotension** — target SBP 80-90 mmHg until surgical hemorrhage control (NOT in TBI — maintain SBP >90)
2. **Hemostatic resuscitation** — 1:1:1 ratio (pRBC:FFP:platelets), minimize crystalloid
3. **Damage control surgery** — abbreviated operation to control hemorrhage and contamination, ICU resuscitation, then definitive repair

### Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP)
- **Activation criteria:** ABC score ≥2, penetrating mechanism, SBP ≤90, HR ≥120, positive FAST
- **1:1:1 ratio** — PROPPR trial (2015, PMID 25647203): reduced exsanguination deaths at 24 hours
- **TXA within 3 hours** — CRASH-2 (2010, PMID 20554319): 1.5% absolute mortality reduction; **harmful if given after 3 hours**

### Lethal Triad
- **Hypothermia + Acidosis + Coagulopathy** — mutually reinforcing
- Present in 25-35% of severely injured patients on arrival
- **Acute traumatic coagulopathy (ATC)** independently associated with 4x increased mortality

## 4. Chest Trauma

### Tension Pneumothorax
- **Clinical diagnosis** — do NOT wait for imaging
- **Findings:** Absent breath sounds, tracheal deviation (AWAY from affected side), JVD, hypotension
- **Treatment:** Immediate needle decompression → chest tube
- **Mechanism:** One-way valve effect → mediastinal shift → obstructive shock (decreased venous return)

### Open Pneumothorax (Sucking Chest Wound)
- **Treatment:** Three-sided occlusive dressing (flutter valve) + chest tube placed AWAY from the wound
- If wound >2/3 tracheal diameter, air preferentially enters wound

### Hemothorax
- **Simple:** <1500 mL, chest tube drainage adequate
- **Massive:** ≥1500 mL initial OR >200 mL/hr x 2-4 hours → **operative thoracotomy**
- **Retained hemothorax:** VATS within 72 hours preferred over fibrinolytics

### Cardiac Tamponade
- **Beck triad:** Hypotension + JVD + muffled heart sounds
- **Pulsus paradoxus:** >10 mmHg drop in SBP with inspiration
- **Diagnosis:** FAST (subxiphoid view), formal echocardiography
- **Treatment:** Pericardiocentesis (bridge) → pericardial window or sternotomy

### Flail Chest
- **Definition:** ≥3 consecutive ribs fractured in ≥2 places → free-floating segment
- **Paradoxical motion** — segment moves inward on inspiration
- **Real killer is underlying pulmonary contusion**, not the mechanical flail
- **Treatment:** Pain control (epidural gold standard), mechanical ventilation if respiratory failure

### Aortic Injury (Traumatic Aortic Disruption)
- **Mechanism:** Rapid deceleration (high-speed MVC, falls >15 feet)
- **Most common site:** Aortic isthmus (just distal to left subclavian — ligamentum arteriosum)
- **CXR clues:** Widened mediastinum (>8 cm), left apical cap, deviation of nasogastric tube
- **Diagnosis:** CT angiography (gold standard)
- **Treatment:** Endovascular repair preferred over open in most cases

### ED Thoracotomy (EDT)
- **Indications:** Penetrating trauma with witnessed cardiac activity/signs of life within 15 min
- **Survival:** Penetrating cardiac = ~15%, penetrating non-cardiac = ~11%, blunt = ~1.5%
- **Contraindication:** Blunt trauma with >5 min CPR or no witnessed arrest
- **Procedure:** Left anterolateral thoracotomy → cardiac massage → aortic cross-clamp → pericardiotomy

## 5. Abdominal Trauma

### FAST Exam
- **4 views:** RUQ (Morison's pouch), LUQ (splenorenal), pelvis (suprapubic), subxiphoid (cardiac)
- **Sensitivity:** ~73% (operator dependent); **Specificity:** ~96%
- **Positive FAST + hemodynamically unstable** → immediate laparotomy (do NOT get CT)
- **Limitations:** Misses retroperitoneal injuries, hollow viscus injuries, small amounts of free fluid

### Blunt Abdominal Trauma
- **Hemodynamically unstable + positive FAST** → OR (not CT)
- **Hemodynamically stable** → CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast
- **Splenic injury:** NOM succeeds in 85% of grade I-III; splenorrhaphy or splenectomy for grades IV-V or instability
  - Post-splenectomy: **vaccinate** (Pneumococcus, Meningococcus, H. influenzae) + lifelong infection risk
- **Hepatic injury:** NOM succeeds in 92% of grade I-III; angioembolization for grade IV
- **Seatbelt sign:** High suspicion for hollow viscus injury (bowel perforation) and mesenteric injury

### Penetrating Abdominal Trauma
- **Gunshot wound to abdomen** → exploratory laparotomy (nearly always)
- **Stab wound:** If hemodynamically unstable → OR; if stable → local wound exploration
  - If anterior stab penetrates fascia → CT or diagnostic laparoscopy
  - If posterior/flank → triple-contrast CT
- **Evisceration** → OR regardless of stability

### Penetrating Neck Trauma
- **Zone I** (clavicles to cricoid): Vascular structures → CTA, possible angiography
- **Zone II** (cricoid to angle of mandible): Most injuries → historically mandatory exploration, now selective with CTA
- **Zone III** (above angle of mandible): Internal carotid → angiography, interventional radiology
- **Hard signs** (active hemorrhage, expanding hematoma, airway compromise, air bubbling) → **immediate OR**

### Retroperitoneal Zones (at Laparotomy)
- **Zone 1** (central/midline): Almost always explore (aorta, IVC, pancreas)
- **Zone 2** (lateral/perirenal): Explore if penetrating; observe if blunt (unless expanding)
- **Zone 3** (pelvic): Do NOT explore (pelvic fracture bleeding — opens tamponade); angioembolization instead

## 6. Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

### Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

| Component | Response | Score |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Eye** | Spontaneous / To voice / To pain / None | 4/3/2/1 |
| **Verbal** | Oriented / Confused / Inappropriate / Incomprehensible / None | 5/4/3/2/1 |
| **Motor** | Obeys / Localizes / Withdraws / Flexion / Extension / None | 6/5/4/3/2/1 |

- **Severe TBI:** GCS ≤8 → intubate, ICP monitor
- **Moderate TBI:** GCS 9-12
- **Mild TBI:** GCS 13-15
- **Motor component** is most prognostic

### Epidural vs Subdural Hematoma

| Feature | Epidural | Subdural |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Source** | Middle meningeal artery (usually) | Bridging veins |
| **CT shape** | Biconvex (lens-shaped) | Crescent-shaped |
| **Classic history** | Lucid interval then rapid decline | Gradual decline or acute |
| **Population** | Young, temporal bone fracture | Elderly, anticoagulated, alcoholics, brain atrophy |
| **Surgical threshold** | >1 cm thickness or >30 mL or midline shift | >10 mm thickness or >5 mm midline shift |
| **Prognosis** | Good if early evacuation | Worse overall (40-60% mortality in acute) |

### Herniation Syndromes
- **Uncal (transtentorial):** CNIII palsy (ipsilateral fixed/dilated pupil) → contralateral hemiparesis → rapid deterioration
- **Central:** Bilateral small then fixed pupils, decorticate → decerebrate posturing
- **Cushing triad:** Hypertension + bradycardia + irregular respirations = **imminent death from brainstem herniation**

### ICP Management (BTF 4th Edition Guidelines)
- **ICP threshold:** <22 mmHg (formerly 20)
- **CPP target:** 60-70 mmHg
- **Tier 1:** HOB 30 degrees, normocapnia (PaCO2 35-40), normothermia, sedation, CSF drainage
- **Tier 2:** Osmotherapy — hypertonic saline (23.4%) or mannitol (0.25-1 g/kg); mild hyperventilation (PaCO2 30-35) as bridge
- **Tier 3:** Decompressive craniectomy, pentobarbital coma

### Secondary Injury Prevention (Shelf Loves This)
- **Hypotension (SBP <90) doubles TBI mortality** — maintain adequate MAP
- **Hypoxia (SpO2 <90) independently worsens outcomes**
- **Avoid hyperventilation** — causes cerebral vasoconstriction and ischemia (only as emergency bridge for herniation)
- **Monroe-Kellie doctrine:** Skull is a fixed box — brain + blood + CSF = constant volume; small volume changes cause exponential ICP rises

## 7. Spinal Injuries

### C-Spine Clearance Decision Rules

| Tool | Sensitivity | Specificity | Key Feature |
|------|------------|-------------|-------------|
| **NEXUS** | 99.6% | 12.9% | 5 low-risk criteria; if ALL met, no imaging needed |
| **Canadian C-Spine Rule** | 99.4% (some studies 100%) | 45.1% | 3-step algorithm; MORE specific than NEXUS |

- **NEXUS 5 criteria:** No posterior midline tenderness, no intoxication, normal alertness (GCS 15), no focal neurologic deficit, no distracting injury
- **CCR preferred** when applicable (alert, stable, age ≥16)
- **Obtunded patient:** High-quality CT alone is sufficient to clear c-spine (EAST guideline, NPV >99%)

### Spinal Cord Syndromes

| Syndrome | Mechanism | Deficit | Preserved |
|----------|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Anterior cord** | Burst fracture, aortic injury | Motor, pain, temperature | Proprioception, vibration (posterior columns) |
| **Central cord** | Hyperextension (elderly, stenosis) | Arms > legs (cape distribution) | Sacral sensation often spared |
| **Brown-Sequard** | Hemisection (penetrating) | Ipsilateral motor + proprioception; contralateral pain/temp | Contralateral motor |
| **Cauda equina** | L1-L2+ compression | Saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction, LMN signs | Variable — surgical emergency |

### Neurogenic Shock vs Spinal Shock

| Feature | Neurogenic Shock | Spinal Shock |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| **Mechanism** | Loss of sympathetic tone | Transient loss of spinal cord function |
| **Level** | Cervical/upper thoracic (above T6) | Any level |
| **Hemodynamics** | **Hypotension + bradycardia** (warm, vasodilated) | Variable |
| **Duration** | Days to weeks | Hours to weeks |
| **Treatment** | Vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line), MAP >85 x 7 days | Supportive, monitor for recovery |
| **Resolution marker** | Hemodynamic normalization | Return of bulbocavernosus reflex |
| **Key distinction** | Cardiovascular emergency | Neurological phenomenon |

### ASIA Impairment Scale
- **A** = Complete (no motor/sensory below level) — <5% motor recovery
- **B** = Sensory incomplete — ~30% motor recovery
- **C** = Motor incomplete (most key muscles <3/5)
- **D** = Motor incomplete (most key muscles ≥3/5)
- **E** = Normal

### High-Yield Shelf Points
- **Neurogenic shock** = hypotension + BRADYCARDIA (unlike hypovolemic shock which has tachycardia)
- **Steroid controversy:** NASCIS II/III legacy — high-dose methylprednisolone NOT recommended by current guidelines (AANS/CNS recommend against)
- **Surgical timing:** Growing evidence for <24h decompression in incomplete SCI

## 8. Burns

### Burn Depth Classification

| Depth | Old Term | Appearance | Sensation | Healing |
|-------|----------|------------|-----------|---------|
| Superficial | 1st degree | Red, dry, no blisters | Painful | 3-5 days, no scar |
| Superficial partial | 2nd degree (superficial) | Pink, moist, blisters, blanches | Very painful | 2-3 weeks, minimal scar |
| Deep partial | 2nd degree (deep) | White/red, mottled, less blanching | Reduced pain | 3-8 weeks, scarring likely |
| Full thickness | 3rd degree | White/brown/black, leathery, no blanching | **Painless** (nerves destroyed) | No spontaneous healing — requires grafting |
| Subdermal | 4th degree | Charred, involves muscle/bone | Painless | Requires excision/amputation |

### Rule of Nines (Adult)

| Region | % TBSA |
|--------|--------|
| Head | 9% |
| Each arm | 9% |
| Anterior trunk | 18% |
| Posterior trunk | 18% |
| Each leg | 18% |
| Perineum | 1% |
| **Total** | **100%** |

- **Pediatric:** Head = 18% (larger proportionally), legs = 14% each
- **Palm method:** Patient's palm ≈ 1% TBSA (for scattered burns)

### Parkland Formula
- **4 mL × weight (kg) × %TBSA** = total fluid for first 24 hours (LR only)
- **Half in first 8 hours** (from time of burn, NOT from arrival)
- **Remaining half over next 16 hours**
- Titrate to **urine output 0.5-1 mL/kg/hr** (adults) or 1-2 mL/kg/hr (children)
- Over-resuscitation → abdominal compartment syndrome, orbital compartment syndrome

### Burn Center Transfer Criteria (ABA)
- Partial thickness >10% TBSA
- Full thickness any size
- Burns to face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, major joints
- Chemical or electrical burns
- Inhalation injury
- Circumferential burns
- Pre-existing medical conditions complicating management

### Inhalation Injury
- **Suspect when:** Enclosed-space fire, facial burns, singed nasal hairs, carbonaceous sputum, hoarse voice
- **Three types:** Thermal (upper airway), chemical (lower airway), systemic toxicity (CO, cyanide)
- **Management:** Early intubation (edema progresses rapidly), bronchoscopy for diagnosis
- **Carbon monoxide:** Cherry-red skin, headache, confusion; COHb >20% = significant; treat with 100% O2 (reduces CO half-life from 250 min to 40-60 min); hyperbaric O2 controversial
- **Cyanide:** Suspect if refractory lactic acidosis despite adequate oxygenation; treat with hydroxocobalamin

### Escharotomy
- **Indication:** Circumferential full-thickness burn causing compartment syndrome or vascular compromise
- **Technique:** Mid-axial incision through eschar only (not fascia — that's fasciotomy)
- **Chest escharotomy** for circumferential chest burns restricting ventilation

### High-Yield Shelf Burn Pearls
- **Electrical burns:** Damage far worse than surface appearance suggests; cardiac monitoring required (arrhythmias); rhabdomyolysis → aggressive hydration + alkalinization; check for compartment syndrome
- **Chemical burns:** Copious water irrigation (>20 min); do NOT try to neutralize
- **Full-thickness burns are PAINLESS** — the classic shelf trick question

## 9. Pelvic Fractures

### Classification
- **Anterior-posterior compression (APC/open book):** External rotation force → pubic symphysis diastasis → bleeding
- **Lateral compression (LC):** Most common; side-impact MVC → internal rotation → less vascular injury
- **Vertical shear (VS):** Fall from height → hemipelvis displaced vertically → highest mortality

### Management Algorithm
- **Hemodynamically unstable + pelvic fracture** → pelvic binder (closes the "open book")
- **Positive FAST** → OR for laparotomy (NOT pelvic packing first)
- **Negative FAST + still unstable** → angioembolization or preperitoneal pelvic packing
- **Do NOT explore pelvic hematoma at laparotomy** (Zone 3) — releases tamponade effect, worsens bleeding
- **Massive hemorrhage source:** Internal iliac vessels and presacral venous plexus

### Associated Injuries
- **Urethral injury:** Blood at meatus, high-riding prostate, scrotal hematoma → retrograde urethrogram BEFORE Foley
- **Bladder rupture:** Gross hematuria with pelvic fracture → CT cystogram
  - Intraperitoneal → operative repair
  - Extraperitoneal → Foley catheter drainage (usually heals)

## 10. Orthopedic Trauma Emergencies

### Compartment Syndrome
- **Mechanism:** Increased pressure in closed fascial compartment → compromised perfusion → ischemia → necrosis
- **Most common:** Tibial shaft fracture (anterior compartment of leg)
- **Clinical findings (6 Ps):** Pain out of proportion, Pain with passive stretch (EARLIEST and most reliable), Paresthesias, Paralysis, Pallor, Pulselessness (LATE — do NOT wait for this)
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical; compartment pressure >30 mmHg or within 30 mmHg of diastolic (delta pressure <30)
- **Treatment:** Emergent fasciotomy — 4-compartment release in lower leg
- **Time-sensitive:** Irreversible damage at 6 hours of ischemia

### Open Fractures (Gustilo Classification)

| Type | Wound | Contamination | Treatment |
|------|-------|---------------|-----------|
| I | <1 cm, clean | Minimal | Cefazolin |
| II | 1-10 cm, moderate tissue damage | Moderate | Cefazolin |
| IIIA | >10 cm, adequate soft tissue | Significant | Cefazolin + aminoglycoside |
| IIIB | >10 cm, inadequate coverage | Significant | Cefazolin + aminoglycoside + flap |
| IIIC | Any with vascular injury needing repair | Any | Cefazolin + aminoglycoside + vascular repair |

- **All open fractures:** Tetanus prophylaxis, antibiotics within 1 hour, wound irrigation, operative debridement within 6-24 hours
- **Gustilo IIIC** has highest amputation rate

### Fat Embolism Syndrome
- **Timing:** 24-72 hours post-fracture (most common with long bone/pelvic fractures)
- **Classic triad:** Respiratory distress + neurologic changes (confusion) + petechial rash (chest, axillae, conjunctivae)
- **Pathophysiology:** Fat globules from marrow enter venous system → lungs → systemic (crosses PFO or overwhelms pulmonary filter)
- **Treatment:** Supportive (O2, mechanical ventilation if needed); early fixation of long bone fractures reduces risk

## 11. Trauma in Special Populations

### Pediatric Trauma
- **#1 cause of death** in children >1 year
- **Larger head:body ratio** → more TBI proportionally
- **More compliant chest wall** → internal organ injury WITHOUT rib fractures (rib fractures in a child = high-force injury, suspect abuse)
- **Higher BSA:weight ratio** → hypothermia risk
- **Fluid resuscitation:** 20 mL/kg boluses of LR
- **Non-accidental trauma (NAT):** Suspect with retinal hemorrhages, subdural hematomas, metaphyseal corner fractures, multiple fractures at different stages of healing

### Geriatric Trauma
- **Falls are #1 mechanism** (not MVC)
- **Lower physiologic reserve** → decompensate faster
- **Beta-blockers/pacemakers** mask tachycardia → "normal" HR despite significant hemorrhage
- **Anticoagulation** → lower threshold for CT head, higher bleeding risk
- **Chronic subdural hematoma** — classic in elderly on anticoagulants; may present weeks after minor fall
- **Each additional rib fracture** increases mortality ~19% in patients >65

### Pregnancy & Trauma
- **Best treatment for the fetus = treat the mother**
- **Left lateral decubitus position** after 20 weeks (avoid IVC compression by gravid uterus)
- **Physiologic changes:** Increased HR (15-20 bpm higher), decreased BP in 2nd trimester, increased blood volume (can lose 30-35% before showing signs of shock)
- **Kleihauer-Betke test** for fetomaternal hemorrhage → guides Rh immunoglobulin dosing
- **Perimortem C-section** within 4-5 minutes of cardiac arrest if >24 weeks

## 13. Mapping to TEFv3 Presentations

| TEFv3 Topic | Shelf Topics Covered | Shelf Weight |
|------------|---------------------|--------------|
| **xABCDE Primary Survey** (#1) | ATLS, primary/secondary survey, shock assessment | High (foundational) |
| **Hemorrhagic Shock & Resuscitation** (#2) | Shock classes, DCR, MTP, TXA, lethal triad | High (20-25%) |
| **Blunt Abdominal Trauma** (#3) | FAST, splenic/hepatic NOM, penetrating abdomen | High |
| **Traumatic Brain Injury** (#4) | GCS, epidural/subdural, herniation, ICP, BTF | High |
| **Thoracic Trauma** (#5) | PTX, hemothorax, tamponade, EDT, aortic injury | High |
| **Burns** (#6) | Rule of nines, Parkland, inhalation, escharotomy | Moderate |

### Shelf Gaps NOT Covered by Current 6 Trauma Topics
- **Spinal cord injury syndromes** (anterior, central, Brown-Sequard, cauda equina)
- **C-spine clearance rules** (NEXUS, CCR)
- **Pelvic fracture management** (binder, angioembolization, zone 3 rule)
- **Compartment syndrome / open fractures** (fasciotomy, Gustilo classification)
- **Penetrating neck zones** (I, II, III management)
- **Fat embolism syndrome**
- **Pediatric non-accidental trauma** (NAT patterns)
- **Geriatric trauma specifics** (falls, anticoagulation reversal, chronic subdural)
- **Pregnancy trauma** (left lateral, Kleihauer-Betke, perimortem C-section)

### Recommendation
The 5 handoff presentations + the existing xABCDE topic cover ~65-70% of shelf trauma content. Adding a **"Trauma Potpourri" presentation** covering spinal syndromes, pelvic fractures, compartment syndrome, burns special topics, and special populations would push coverage to ~90%.
