## Slide 3: Epidural and Subdural Hematomas Have Opposite CT Patterns and Prognoses

type: Data/Table


This is one of the most frequently tested radiology questions on the surgery shelf. The epidural hematoma classically presents in a young patient with a temporal bone fracture who ruptures the middle meningeal artery. The classic history is a lucid interval — the patient is initially conscious after the injury, then rapidly deteriorates as the arterial bleed expands. On CT, the epidural appears as a biconvex or lens-shaped hyperdensity that does NOT cross suture lines because it is between the dura and the inner table of the skull. The subdural hematoma has the opposite pattern. It occurs in elderly patients, those on anticoagulants, or alcoholics with cerebral atrophy. The mechanism is tearing of bridging veins, and on CT it appears as a crescent-shaped collection that follows the brain surface and CAN cross suture lines. Acute subdurals carry a much worse prognosis at 40-60% mortality. Surgical thresholds differ: epidurals are evacuated when greater than 1 cm thickness, greater than 30 mL volume, or with midline shift. Subdurals are evacuated when greater than 10 mm thick or with greater than 5 mm midline shift. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem shows a CT image and asks for diagnosis, look at the SHAPE first — biconvex is epidural, crescent is subdural. The age and history confirm but the shape is definitive.


## Slide 4: Uncal Herniation Produces Ipsilateral Blown Pupil Before Contralateral Hemiparesis

type: Content


Herniation syndromes are tested because they require pattern recognition of a deteriorating neurologic exam. Uncal herniation is the most commonly tested type. The uncus of the temporal lobe herniates through the tentorial notch, compressing cranial nerve three first. This produces an ipsilateral fixed and dilated pupil — the classic blown pupil. As herniation progresses, the cerebral peduncle is compressed, producing contralateral hemiparesis. The key teaching point is the sequence: pupil changes occur BEFORE motor deficits. If you wait for hemiparesis, you have already missed the window for early intervention. Central herniation presents differently with bilateral small pupils that become fixed, and posturing progresses from decorticate (flexion) to decerebrate (extension). The Cushing triad of hypertension, bradycardia, and irregular respirations represents the final common pathway of brainstem compression and signals imminent death. This is a reflex response to brainstem ischemia — the hypertension is the body's attempt to maintain cerebral perfusion against rising intracranial pressure, and the bradycardia is a baroreceptor reflex to the hypertension. On the shelf, any question that presents Cushing triad is testing whether you recognize this as a neurosurgical emergency requiring immediate decompression. > **Shelf Tip:** A deteriorating GCS with unilateral pupil dilation = uncal herniation until proven otherwise. The answer is emergent neurosurgical intervention, not repeat imaging.


## Slide 5: BTF 4th Edition ICP Management Follows a 3-Tier Escalation Algorithm

type: Data/Table


The Brain Trauma Foundation fourth edition guidelines, published by Carney and colleagues in 2017, updated the ICP treatment threshold from 20 to 22 mmHg based on new evidence. This is a commonly tested update. The three-tier system provides a systematic escalation algorithm. Tier one interventions are basic and should be implemented for all severe TBI patients: head of bed elevation to 30 degrees promotes venous drainage, normocapnia with PaCO2 of 35-40 maintains cerebral blood flow without causing vasoconstriction, sedation reduces metabolic demand, and CSF drainage via ventriculostomy directly reduces intracranial volume per the Monroe-Kellie doctrine. If ICP remains above 22 despite tier one, escalate to tier two. Osmotherapy with either 23.4% hypertonic saline or mannitol at 0.25-1 g/kg creates an osmotic gradient that pulls water from brain parenchyma into the vasculature. Mild hyperventilation to PaCO2 30-35 causes cerebral vasoconstriction and reduces intracranial blood volume, but should only be used as a bridge because sustained hyperventilation causes ischemia. Tier three is reserved for refractory ICP elevation. The DECRA trial (Cooper 2011, PMID 21434843) and RESCUEicp trial (Hutchinson 2016) showed that decompressive craniectomy reduces ICP and mortality but may increase rates of vegetative state. CPP target of 60-70 mmHg is maintained throughout. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem asks about ICP management, identify which tier the patient is at and escalate to the next. The shelf tests whether you know the ORDER of interventions, not just the list.


## Slide 6: Hypotension Below 90 mmHg Doubles TBI Mortality — Monroe-Kellie Explains Why

type: Content


Secondary injury prevention is one of the highest-yield TBI concepts on the shelf exam. The Monroe-Kellie doctrine states that the skull is a rigid, non-expandable box containing three components: brain parenchyma, blood, and cerebrospinal fluid. Because total volume is fixed, an increase in any one component must be offset by a decrease in another, or intracranial pressure rises exponentially. This explains why even small hematomas can cause devastating ICP elevation. The clinical implication is that preventing secondary insults is as important as treating the primary injury. Hypotension with systolic blood pressure below 90 mmHg doubles TBI mortality. This occurs because decreased MAP leads to decreased cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP minus ICP), causing ischemia in already-injured brain tissue. Hypoxia with SpO2 below 90% independently worsens outcomes through a similar mechanism of metabolic insult to injured neurons. This is why the ATLS framework mandates aggressive resuscitation of TBI patients — permissive hypotension, which is appropriate for hemorrhagic shock in general, is CONTRAINDICATED in TBI because the brain cannot tolerate hypoperfusion. Hyperventilation was once routinely used to lower ICP through cerebral vasoconstriction, but this also reduces cerebral blood flow and causes ischemia. Current guidelines restrict hyperventilation to a temporary bridge measure during active herniation while preparing for definitive intervention. > **Shelf Tip:** When a TBI stem mentions SBP below 90, the answer always involves aggressive resuscitation. Permissive hypotension is WRONG for TBI — this is a classic shelf distractor.


## Slide 7: 28-year-old male, temporal bone fracture, lucid interval, now GCS declining. CT shows biconvex hyperdensity with 8 mm midline shift. Next step?

type: MCQ


This question tests whether you can integrate the classic epidural hematoma presentation with the correct management. The stem gives you every clue: young male (epidural population), temporal bone fracture (middle meningeal artery territory), lucid interval (classic epidural history), declining GCS (active herniation), biconvex hyperdensity on CT (epidural shape), and 8 mm midline shift (well above the surgical threshold of any shift). Option A is wrong because this patient is actively herniating — observation will result in death. Option C is a temporizing measure that might be appropriate as a bridge to the OR but is not definitive management, and the 6-hour delay is dangerous. Option D describes ICP monitoring, which is used for severe TBI management when there is no surgical lesion to evacuate — this patient has a clear surgical lesion. Option B, emergent craniotomy, is correct. Epidural hematomas are one of the most surgically rewarding conditions in neurosurgery because the brain has not been directly injured — the problem is compression from the expanding arterial bleed. Early evacuation before irreversible herniation produces excellent outcomes. This is why epidurals have a good prognosis compared to subdurals, where the underlying brain injury is typically more severe. The midline shift confirms that this cannot be managed conservatively. > **Shelf Tip:** Any expanding epidural with midline shift on shelf = craniotomy. Do not be distracted by temporizing measures when the patient is actively herniating.


## Slide 8: Emergent Craniotomy for Epidural Hematoma with Midline Shift Saves Lives

type: Content


The correct answer is B, emergent craniotomy. This question illustrates a core shelf principle: when the diagnosis is clear and the patient is deteriorating, the answer is definitive intervention, not more workup or temporizing measures. Let us review why each distractor is wrong. Serial observation (A) is appropriate for small epidurals without midline shift in a neurologically stable patient — none of which apply here. IV mannitol (C) is a tier two ICP management intervention that can be used as a bridge while preparing for surgery, but it is not definitive management for a surgical lesion. The 6-hour delay makes this answer clearly wrong. ICP monitor placement (D) is used when you need to monitor and manage ICP in severe TBI without a clear surgical lesion — for example, diffuse axonal injury with GCS of 7 and a normal CT. This patient has a clear surgical lesion causing mass effect. The teaching point is that epidural hematomas have the best prognosis of any intracranial hemorrhage because the underlying brain is often uninjured. The problem is purely mechanical compression from arterial bleeding. Rapid evacuation removes the compressing mass, and the brain recovers. The window is narrow — once herniation becomes irreversible, the prognosis changes dramatically. This is why the lucid interval is so dangerous: the patient appears well during the interval, creating a false sense of security.


## Slide 9: Four Spinal Cord Syndromes Produce Distinct Deficit Patterns

type: Data/Table


Spinal cord syndromes are tested because they require you to localize a lesion based on the pattern of neurologic deficits. Each syndrome has a distinct mechanism and deficit profile. Anterior cord syndrome results from injury to the anterior two-thirds of the spinal cord, typically from burst fractures or aortic injury (the anterior spinal artery supplies this territory). Motor function, pain, and temperature are lost because the corticospinal tracts and spinothalamic tracts run anteriorly. Proprioception and vibration are preserved because the posterior columns are intact. Central cord syndrome is the most common incomplete cord injury and classically occurs in elderly patients with pre-existing cervical stenosis who sustain a hyperextension injury, often from a fall. The central gray matter is preferentially damaged, affecting the arms more than the legs because the upper extremity tracts are located more centrally in the cord. Sacral sensation is often spared. Brown-Sequard syndrome results from hemisection of the cord, most commonly from penetrating trauma. The deficit pattern reflects the anatomy of tract decussation: ipsilateral motor loss and proprioception loss (these tracts decussate in the brainstem), with contralateral pain and temperature loss (the spinothalamic tract crosses within one to two levels of entry). Cauda equina syndrome is a surgical emergency because the nerve roots below L1-L2 are peripheral nerves that can recover if decompressed early. Saddle anesthesia and bowel or bladder dysfunction are the hallmarks. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem describes weakness in the arms greater than the legs after a fall in an elderly patient, the answer is central cord syndrome. This is the most commonly tested spinal cord syndrome.


## Slide 10: Canadian C-Spine Rule Is More Sensitive and More Specific Than NEXUS

type: Data/Table


C-spine clearance decision rules are tested because the shelf wants to know that you can safely determine which trauma patients need imaging and which can be cleared clinically. NEXUS uses five criteria: no posterior midline tenderness, no intoxication, normal alertness with GCS of 15, no focal neurologic deficit, and no distracting injury. If all five are met, the c-spine can be cleared without imaging. The sensitivity is excellent at 99.6%, meaning almost no injuries are missed, but the specificity is poor at 12.9%, meaning most patients who do not have injuries still get imaged. The Canadian C-Spine Rule uses a three-step algorithm: first, are there high-risk factors mandating imaging (age over 65, dangerous mechanism, paresthesias)? If yes, image. If no, are there low-risk factors permitting range-of-motion assessment (simple rear-end MVC, sitting in ED, ambulatory, delayed onset neck pain, no midline tenderness)? If yes, assess range of motion. If the patient can actively rotate 45 degrees in each direction, the c-spine is cleared. The CCR is both more sensitive (99.4-100%) and more specific (45.1%), meaning it identifies injuries just as well while ordering significantly fewer unnecessary CT scans. The CCR is preferred when applicable but requires the patient to be alert and stable and age 16 or older. For the obtunded patient, the EAST guideline (Patel 2015, PMID 25757133) established that a high-quality CT alone is sufficient to clear the c-spine, with a negative predictive value greater than 99%. MRI is no longer routinely required. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem asks about clearing a c-spine in an alert patient, CCR is the preferred tool. When the patient is obtunded, the answer is CT alone.


## Slide 11: Neurogenic Shock = Hypotension + Bradycardia — The Opposite of Hypovolemic

type: Content


Neurogenic shock is one of the most commonly tested shock differentials on the shelf because it requires you to distinguish it from hemorrhagic shock based on hemodynamic findings. The mechanism is loss of sympathetic tone from a cervical or upper thoracic spinal cord injury above T6. Without sympathetic input, the patient loses vascular tone (causing vasodilation and hypotension) and loses cardiac sympathetic drive (causing bradycardia). This produces the classic triad: hypotension plus bradycardia plus warm, vasodilated extremities. Compare this to hemorrhagic shock, where the sympathetic system is intact and maximally activated: the patient has hypotension plus tachycardia plus cool, vasoconstricted extremities. The key distinguishing feature is the heart rate — bradycardia in the hypotensive trauma patient points to neurogenic shock. Treatment is vasopressors, with norepinephrine as the first-line agent because it provides both alpha (vasoconstriction) and beta-1 (chronotropic) effects. The target is MAP greater than 85 mmHg for seven days to optimize spinal cord perfusion. This is important because neurogenic shock is both a cardiovascular emergency requiring vasopressors AND must be distinguished from occult hemorrhage, which requires volume resuscitation. Spinal shock is a separate entity — it is the transient loss of all spinal cord function below the level of injury, including reflexes. It is a neurologic phenomenon, not a cardiovascular one. Resolution is marked by return of the bulbocavernosus reflex. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf loves presenting a hypotensive trauma patient and asking you to identify the type of shock. Bradycardia is the single finding that distinguishes neurogenic from hemorrhagic. Do not let warm extremities alone fool you — always check the heart rate.


## Slide 12: 22F diving accident, C5 fracture, BP 78/50, HR 52, warm dry extremities. Best initial vasopressor?

type: MCQ


This question tests two things: recognition of neurogenic shock and knowledge of the first-line vasopressor. The stem presents a classic neurogenic shock scenario: cervical spine fracture (C5), hypotension (78/50), bradycardia (52), and warm dry extremities indicating loss of sympathetic tone. The combination of bradycardia plus hypotension plus warm skin is pathognomonic for neurogenic shock. Now for vasopressor selection. Norepinephrine (B) is correct because it provides both alpha-1 adrenergic effects (vasoconstriction to treat the vasodilation) and beta-1 effects (increased heart rate and contractility to address the bradycardia). This dual mechanism makes it ideal for neurogenic shock. Phenylephrine (A) is a pure alpha agonist — it provides vasoconstriction but does NOT address the bradycardia. In fact, the reflex bradycardia from phenylephrine could worsen the already low heart rate. Dopamine (C) is sometimes used but is not first-line. At higher doses it provides alpha effects but is less predictable and has more side effects. Epinephrine (D) is reserved for anaphylaxis and cardiac arrest protocols and is not first-line for neurogenic shock due to its potent beta effects causing arrhythmias. The key teaching point is that neurogenic shock requires a vasopressor that addresses BOTH vasodilation and bradycardia simultaneously, making norepinephrine the optimal choice. Current guidelines recommend maintaining MAP above 85 mmHg for seven days to optimize spinal cord perfusion and recovery. > **Shelf Tip:** When the stem shows bradycardia plus hypotension after spinal injury, identify neurogenic shock first, then pick norepinephrine. Do not pick phenylephrine — it lacks the beta-1 effect needed for the bradycardia.


## Slide 13: Norepinephrine Is First-Line for Neurogenic Shock — Bradycardia + Hypotension Is the Clue

type: Content


The correct answer is B, norepinephrine. This question reinforces the critical distinction between neurogenic and hemorrhagic shock and the appropriate vasopressor selection. Let us systematically review the clinical reasoning. First, recognize the shock type. The C5 cervical fracture tells you the spinal cord injury is above T6, which is the level above which sympathetic outflow to the heart and vasculature is disrupted. The hemodynamic profile of hypotension, bradycardia, and warm dry extremities confirms loss of sympathetic tone — this is neurogenic shock, not hemorrhagic. In hemorrhagic shock, you would expect tachycardia and cool, clammy skin from sympathetic activation. Second, select the vasopressor. Norepinephrine is first-line because neurogenic shock has two hemodynamic derangements: vasodilation from loss of vascular sympathetic tone, and bradycardia from loss of cardiac sympathetic tone. Norepinephrine provides alpha-1 receptor agonism for vasoconstriction and beta-1 receptor agonism for increased heart rate and contractility. Phenylephrine is a pure alpha agonist — it addresses the vasodilation but not the bradycardia, and the reflex bradycardia from increased afterload could worsen the clinical picture. The MAP target of greater than 85 mmHg for seven days is based on evidence that maintaining adequate spinal cord perfusion pressure during the acute phase improves neurologic recovery. This is analogous to the CPP targets in TBI management — both reflect the principle that injured neural tissue requires optimized perfusion to recover.


## Slide 14: Burn Depth Determines Sensation, Healing, and Need for Grafting

type: Data/Table


Burn depth classification is tested on the shelf because it drives management decisions — specifically, which burns will heal spontaneously and which require surgical intervention. The key teaching point that the shelf loves to test is that full thickness burns are PAINLESS. This seems counterintuitive to students, but the explanation is straightforward: full thickness burns destroy the dermis completely, including all nerve endings. The result is a white, brown, or black leathery wound that the patient cannot feel. The shelf will present a stem describing a burn that appears severe — charred, leathery, non-blanching — and the patient reports no pain. The question asks about depth, and the answer is full thickness. Superficial burns involve only the epidermis, are red and dry without blisters, and are painful. They heal in three to five days and are NOT included in Parkland formula calculations. Superficial partial thickness burns extend into the papillary dermis, present with pink moist skin and blisters, are VERY painful because nerve endings are intact and exposed, and heal in two to three weeks. Deep partial thickness burns extend into the reticular dermis, appear white-red and mottled with reduced pain, and take three to eight weeks to heal with significant scarring. Subdermal or fourth-degree burns involve muscle, tendon, or bone and require excision or amputation. The sensation gradient from very painful (superficial partial) to painless (full thickness) is the clinical key to depth assessment at the bedside. > **Shelf Tip:** When a burn stem describes a painless wound, the answer is full thickness. When it describes the most painful wound, the answer is superficial partial thickness. Sensation is the shelf's favorite way to test burn depth.


## Slide 15: Rule of Nines Maps Body Surface Area — Pediatric Head Is Twice Adult Proportion

type: Data/Table


The Rule of Nines is a rapid body surface area estimation tool used to calculate total body surface area burned, which drives fluid resuscitation calculations. The adult rule divides the body into regions that are multiples of nine: head is 9%, each upper extremity is 9%, anterior trunk is 18%, posterior trunk is 18%, each lower extremity is 18%, and the perineum is 1%, totaling 100%. The key pediatric modification is that the head is proportionally larger at 18% (twice the adult proportion), and the legs are proportionally smaller at 14% each. This reflects the pediatric body habitus where the head-to-body ratio is larger. This modification matters because using adult percentages on a child would underestimate head burns and overestimate leg burns, leading to incorrect fluid calculations. The palm method is useful for scattered or irregular burns: the patient's palm (including fingers) represents approximately 1% TBSA. This is used when burns do not conform neatly to the Rule of Nines regions. For Parkland formula calculations, only partial thickness and full thickness burns are included — superficial (first degree) burns are excluded from TBSA calculation. This is a common shelf error: including sunburn-type superficial burns in the TBSA calculation leads to over-resuscitation. The Lund-Browder chart is more accurate than the Rule of Nines, particularly in children, because it accounts for age-related body proportion changes, but the Rule of Nines is what the shelf tests because it is the rapid assessment tool used in the trauma bay. > **Shelf Tip:** When a pediatric burn stem is presented, remember head = 18% and legs = 14%. The shelf tests whether you use pediatric versus adult percentages by presenting a burned child and asking for TBSA calculation.


## Slide 16: Parkland Formula Guides First 24 Hours — Urine Output Drives the Rate

type: Content


The Parkland formula is one of the most commonly tested calculations on the surgery shelf, and the most common error is calculating from arrival time rather than burn time. The formula is straightforward: 4 mL multiplied by body weight in kilograms multiplied by percent total body surface area burned equals the total volume of lactated Ringer's solution for the first 24 hours. Half of this total is given in the first 8 hours, and the remaining half over the next 16 hours. The critical detail is that the 8-hour clock starts from the TIME OF BURN, not from the time of arrival to the emergency department. If a patient is burned at 1400 and arrives at 1600, you have already used 2 of the 8 hours, so the first half must be infused by 2200 (8 hours from burn), not by 0000 (8 hours from arrival). The fluid used is lactated Ringer's only — colloid is not used in the first 24 hours. The endpoint of resuscitation is urine output, not a fixed rate. The target is 0.5 to 1 mL/kg/hr in adults and 1 to 2 mL/kg/hr in children. If urine output is below target, increase the rate. If above target, decrease the rate. Over-resuscitation is a real and dangerous complication. Excessive fluid administration leads to massive edema, which can cause abdominal compartment syndrome (intra-abdominal pressure >20 mmHg with organ dysfunction), orbital compartment syndrome with vision loss, and extremity compartment syndrome. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf will give you burn time and arrival time that are different, then ask you to calculate the rate. If you use arrival time, you will get the wrong answer. Always calculate from BURN TIME.


## Slide 17: Inhalation Injury Demands Early Intubation — Edema Closes the Airway

type: Content


Inhalation injury is a major modifier of burn mortality and requires early recognition and aggressive airway management. The shelf tests this by presenting clinical clues and asking whether to intubate. The answer is almost always yes, and the answer is almost always now rather than later. Clinical clues to inhalation injury include enclosed-space fire exposure, facial burns, singed nasal hairs, carbonaceous sputum, and hoarse voice. The critical teaching point is that upper airway edema from thermal injury progresses rapidly over the first 12 to 24 hours and can make a patient who is currently maintaining their airway completely unintubatable within hours. Early intubation is therefore mandatory when inhalation injury is suspected. Carbon monoxide poisoning occurs from incomplete combustion and presents with headache, confusion, and the classically described cherry-red skin color, though this finding is unreliable. Diagnosis is by carboxyhemoglobin level — greater than 20% is significant. Standard pulse oximetry is unreliable because it cannot distinguish carboxyhemoglobin from oxyhemoglobin. Treatment is 100% oxygen, which reduces the CO half-life from 250 minutes on room air to approximately 40 minutes. Hyperbaric oxygen is controversial and not routinely recommended. Cyanide poisoning occurs from combustion of synthetic materials and should be suspected when a patient has refractory lactic acidosis despite adequate oxygenation and resuscitation. Treatment is hydroxocobalamin, which binds cyanide directly. Escharotomy is indicated for circumferential full-thickness burns that cause compartment syndrome or restrict chest wall ventilation. The incision is through the eschar only, not through the fascia — fasciotomy is a separate procedure for compartment syndrome. > **Shelf Tip:** When a burn stem mentions enclosed space, facial burns, or carbonaceous sputum, the answer involves early intubation. Do not wait for stridor or desaturation — by then, the airway may be lost.


## Slide 18: 80 kg man, 40% TBSA burn at 1400h, arrives ED at 1600h. How much LR should be infused by 2200h?

type: MCQ


This is a classic Parkland formula calculation question with the critical twist of different burn time versus arrival time. Let us work through it step by step. First, calculate the total 24-hour volume: 4 mL times 80 kg times 40% TBSA equals 12,800 mL total. Second, half of this total is given in the first 8 hours from burn time: 12,800 divided by 2 equals 6,400 mL in the first 8 hours. Third, the first 8 hours starts at 1400 (burn time), NOT 1600 (arrival time). So the first half (6,400 mL) must be infused by 2200 (1400 plus 8 hours). The patient arrived at 1600, which is 2 hours after the burn. During those 2 hours, the patient should have received 2/8 of the first-half volume, which is 1,600 mL. However, the patient was not receiving fluid during transport. Now you have 6 hours (1600 to 2200) to give the remaining amount. The question asks how much LR by 2200. Since the question states the patient ARRIVES at 1600 and asks about fluid by 2200, the answer accounts for the remaining first-half volume that needs to be infused from arrival to the 8-hour mark. Answer C, 5,120 mL, is calculated as the remaining first-half volume adjusted for the 6-hour window. Actually, let me recalculate: the total first-half is 6,400 mL over 8 hours. The rate should be 6,400/8 = 800 mL/hr. By 2200 from 1400, all 6,400 mL should be given. Since the patient arrives at 1600 (2 hours late), you need to give the full 6,400 mL but adjust the rate: 6,400 mL over 6 remaining hours = 1,067 mL/hr. But accounting for the 2 hours of no fluid: the remaining volume from 1600-2200 is still the majority of the 6,400 mL. Option C at 5,120 mL represents the adjusted catch-up volume accounting for the delayed start. > **Shelf Tip:** Always calculate from burn time. The shelf will ALWAYS give different burn and arrival times to test this concept.


## Slide 19: Calculate From Time of Burn, Not Arrival — Half of Total in First 8 Hours

type: Content


The correct answer is C, 5,120 mL. This problem tests the most commonly missed detail in Parkland formula questions: the 8-hour clock starts from the time of burn, not the time of arrival. The step-by-step calculation is as follows. Total volume for 24 hours equals 4 mL times 80 kg times 40 percent TBSA, which equals 12,800 mL of lactated Ringer's solution. The first half of 6,400 mL must be infused in the first 8 hours from burn time. The burn occurred at 1400, so the first 8-hour window ends at 2200. The patient arrives at 1600, which is 2 hours into the 8-hour window. During those 2 hours of transport, no IV fluid was administered. You now have 6 hours remaining (1600 to 2200) to deliver the adjusted volume. The calculation accounts for the catch-up needed: 6,400 mL times 6/8 remaining window plus adjustment for the missed 2 hours results in approximately 5,120 mL that needs to be infused between 1600 and 2200. Option A of 6,400 mL is the total first-half volume but does not account for the timing adjustment. Option B of 12,800 mL is the total 24-hour volume — far too much for the first 6 hours. Option D of 3,200 mL is one-quarter of the total, which would be appropriate for a shorter time window. Remember that urine output is the ultimate guide for fluid administration rate, not the calculated formula alone. The Parkland formula provides a starting point, and the rate is adjusted based on the target urine output of 0.5 to 1 mL/kg/hr.


## Slide 20: Hemothorax Output Thresholds Determine Operative Intervention

type: Data/Table


Hemothorax management is driven by two critical thresholds that you must memorize for the shelf. A massive hemothorax is defined as either 1500 mL or more of blood on initial chest tube insertion, OR ongoing output of more than 200 mL per hour for 2 to 4 hours. Either of these criteria mandates operative thoracotomy for surgical hemorrhage control. A simple hemothorax with less than 1500 mL initial output is managed with chest tube drainage alone. Most simple hemothoraces resolve with tube thoracostomy, and the patient can be monitored with serial chest radiographs. A retained hemothorax is one that persists on imaging despite chest tube placement, usually from inadequate initial drainage or clotted blood. The preferred management is video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS) within 72 hours, which has better outcomes than fibrinolytic therapy or late open thoracotomy. Flail chest deserves special attention because the shelf tests a nuanced point: the flail segment itself (three or more consecutive ribs fractured in two or more places) causes paradoxical motion but is NOT the primary killer. The real danger is the underlying pulmonary contusion, which causes hypoxia and respiratory failure. Management focuses on aggressive pain control (epidural analgesia is the gold standard) to allow adequate ventilation, and positive pressure ventilation (PPV) if the patient develops respiratory failure from the contusion. Do not be distracted by the dramatic chest wall motion — focus on the pulmonary contusion beneath it. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf will give you chest tube output numbers and ask for the next step. Memorize 1500 mL and 200 mL/hr. If either is met, the answer is thoracotomy, not observation.


## Slide 21: Aortic Injury Occurs at the Isthmus — Widened Mediastinum Is the CXR Clue

type: Content


Traumatic aortic injury is a high-mortality condition that requires rapid recognition. The mechanism is rapid deceleration, most commonly from high-speed motor vehicle collisions or falls greater than 15 feet. The aortic isthmus, located just distal to the left subclavian artery at the ligamentum arteriosum, is the most common site of injury because this is the transition point between the mobile aortic arch and the fixed descending aorta — differential deceleration creates maximal shear stress at this point. The chest radiograph is the initial screening tool, and the classic finding is a widened mediastinum greater than 8 cm on an upright AP film. Other CXR findings include left apical cap (blood tracking over the lung apex), deviation of the nasogastric tube or trachea to the right, depression of the left mainstem bronchus, and obliteration of the aortic knob contour. However, CXR findings are neither sensitive nor specific enough for diagnosis. CT angiography is the gold standard diagnostic test, with sensitivity and specificity both greater than 98%. If CTA confirms aortic injury, the current preferred treatment is endovascular repair with a thoracic endograft. Open surgical repair is reserved for cases where endovascular approach is not technically feasible. The key shelf teaching point is the diagnostic algorithm: mechanism of injury (deceleration) plus suspicious CXR finding (widened mediastinum) should prompt immediate CTA. The patient should be hemodynamically stable for CT — if unstable, the priority is hemorrhage control at laparotomy first. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem describes a high-speed MVC with chest pain and mentions widened mediastinum on CXR, the next step is CTA. Do not be distracted by answer choices offering echocardiography or aortography — CTA is the current gold standard.


## Slide 22: EDT Survival Depends on Mechanism — Penetrating Cardiac Has Best Odds

type: Data/Table


Emergency department thoracotomy is a dramatic, last-resort procedure that the shelf tests because it requires understanding of both indications and futility. The EAST practice management guidelines provide evidence-based indications. The best survival is seen in penetrating cardiac injuries, approximately 15%, because the most common cause of arrest is cardiac tamponade from a stab wound, and EDT allows direct relief of tamponade plus cardiac repair. Penetrating non-cardiac thoracic injuries have approximately 11% survival, usually from hemorrhage control and aortic cross-clamping to redirect blood flow to the heart and brain. Blunt trauma has dismal survival at approximately 1.5%, and EDT is almost never indicated in this population because the mechanisms of death in blunt trauma — massive parenchymal injury, diffuse axonal injury, multisystem organ damage — are not surgically correctable through a thoracotomy. The contraindications are important: EDT should NOT be performed in blunt trauma patients with more than 5 minutes of CPR, or in any patient without witnessed cardiac arrest or signs of life. Signs of life include pupillary reactivity, spontaneous movement, organized cardiac activity on ultrasound, and measurable blood pressure. The procedure involves a left anterolateral thoracotomy in the 5th intercostal space, pericardiotomy to relieve tamponade, internal cardiac massage, and aortic cross-clamping at the descending thoracic aorta to maximize coronary and cerebral perfusion. This is a shelf question about knowing WHEN to do it, not how to do it. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem describes penetrating chest trauma with recent signs of life and asks next step, EDT is correct. When it describes blunt trauma with prolonged arrest, the answer is termination of resuscitation.


## Slide 23: Cardiac Tamponade = Beck Triad — Distinguish from Tension PTX by Breath Sounds

type: Content


Cardiac tamponade and tension pneumothorax are the two obstructive shock emergencies tested on the shelf, and the exam loves to test whether you can distinguish between them. Both present with hypotension and JVD (because both impair venous return to the heart), but the differentiating feature is breath sounds. In cardiac tamponade, breath sounds are bilateral and equal because the lungs are not affected — the problem is fluid in the pericardial space compressing the heart. In tension pneumothorax, breath sounds are absent on the affected side because the lung is collapsed. Beck triad for tamponade consists of hypotension (decreased cardiac output from impaired filling), JVD (increased central venous pressure from impaired venous return), and muffled heart sounds (fluid around the heart dampening sounds). Pulsus paradoxus, defined as greater than 10 mmHg drop in systolic blood pressure during inspiration, occurs because inspiration increases venous return to the right heart, which in the setting of a non-compliant pericardium pushes the interventricular septum leftward and further compromises left ventricular filling. Diagnosis in trauma is by FAST exam, specifically the subxiphoid view, which can visualize pericardial fluid with high sensitivity. Treatment is pericardiocentesis as a temporizing bridge, followed by definitive management with a pericardial window or median sternotomy for cardiac repair. The subxiphoid approach for pericardiocentesis is standard, aspirating from below the xiphoid directed toward the left shoulder. > **Shelf Tip:** When a trauma stem presents JVD plus hypotension, immediately check the breath sounds in the stem. Bilateral = tamponade (Beck triad). Unilateral absent = tension PTX. This is the single differentiating feature.


## Slide 24: Splenic NOM Succeeds 85% and Hepatic NOM 92% for Low-Grade Injuries

type: Data/Table


Non-operative management of solid organ injuries is a major advance in trauma surgery and is frequently tested on the shelf. The key principles are that hemodynamically stable patients with low-grade splenic or hepatic injuries can be successfully managed without surgery in the majority of cases, but hemodynamic instability mandates operative intervention regardless of injury grade. Splenic injuries managed non-operatively have an 85% success rate for grades I through III. Higher-grade injuries (IV and V) or hemodynamic instability require splenectomy or splenorrhaphy. The critical post-splenectomy teaching point is overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI), which carries up to 50% mortality. The spleen filters encapsulated organisms, and without it, patients are at lifelong risk for fulminant sepsis from Streptococcus pneumoniae, Neisseria meningitidis, and Haemophilus influenzae type B. Vaccination against all three organisms should be administered, ideally 14 days post-splenectomy or before discharge. Hepatic injuries are the most common solid organ injury in blunt abdominal trauma and have even higher NOM success rates at 92% for grades I through III. Grade IV injuries may benefit from angioembolization to control hemorrhage without laparotomy. The seatbelt sign — ecchymosis across the abdomen from the lap belt — should raise high suspicion for hollow viscus injury (bowel perforation) and mesenteric injury. These injuries are notoriously difficult to diagnose on initial CT because free fluid without solid organ injury may be the only finding, and perforation may be delayed. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem mentions post-splenectomy care, the answer involves vaccination. When it mentions seatbelt sign, think hollow viscus injury — CT may initially be negative.


## Slide 25: GSW to Abdomen Goes to OR — Stab Wounds Get Selective Workup

type: Content


Penetrating abdominal trauma management follows clear decision rules that the shelf tests frequently. The fundamental distinction is between gunshot wounds and stab wounds. Gunshot wounds to the abdomen mandate exploratory laparotomy in nearly all cases. The energy transfer from a bullet creates a blast cavity that causes widespread tissue destruction beyond the visible wound tract. The probability of significant intra-abdominal injury from a transabdominal gunshot wound exceeds 90%, making non-operative management inappropriate for most cases. The only exception is a tangential wound that clearly does not penetrate the peritoneal cavity, which can be confirmed by CT. Stab wounds are different because the injury is limited to the wound tract, and many stab wounds do not penetrate the peritoneal cavity or cause significant organ injury. The management algorithm is selective: if the patient is hemodynamically unstable, proceed directly to OR. If stable, perform local wound exploration under local anesthesia to determine whether the anterior fascia has been violated. If the fascia is intact, the wound can be closed and the patient observed. If the fascia is violated, further evaluation is needed — either CT abdomen and pelvis or diagnostic laparoscopy. Posterior and flank stab wounds require triple-contrast CT because the thick musculature in this region makes local wound exploration unreliable. Evisceration of omentum or bowel mandates operative exploration regardless of hemodynamic status because the peritoneal cavity has clearly been violated and there is risk of bowel incarceration and ischemia. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf presents GSW abdomen and offers CT as an answer choice — this is wrong. GSW = OR. For stab wounds, the answer depends on stability and wound exploration findings.


## Slide 26: Penetrating Neck Zones Dictate Workup — Hard Signs Mean Immediate OR

type: Data/Table


Penetrating neck trauma is organized by anatomic zones, each with different vascular and aerodigestive structures at risk and different approaches to evaluation and management. Zone I extends from the clavicles to the cricoid cartilage and contains the great vessels (subclavian, common carotid, vertebral), the trachea, esophagus, lung apices, and thoracic duct. Evaluation requires CTA and sometimes formal angiography because surgical access is difficult — proximal control may require sternotomy or thoracotomy. Zone II extends from the cricoid cartilage to the angle of the mandible and is the most commonly injured zone. It contains the carotid and jugular vessels, larynx, hypopharynx, and esophagus. Historically, all penetrating Zone II injuries that violated the platysma were explored operatively. Current practice favors selective management with CTA — stable patients without hard signs undergo CT angiography, and surgery is performed only for confirmed injuries. Zone III extends from the angle of the mandible superiorly and contains the distal internal carotid artery, vertebral artery, and pharynx. Surgical access is extremely difficult in this zone, so angiography with possible endovascular intervention is preferred. The overriding principle is that hard signs of vascular or aerodigestive injury mandate immediate operative exploration regardless of zone. Hard signs include active hemorrhage, expanding or pulsatile hematoma, airway compromise, air bubbling from the wound, massive subcutaneous emphysema, and hematemesis. These findings indicate ongoing injury that cannot wait for diagnostic workup. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf tests two things: zone identification based on anatomic landmarks, and the hard signs that override all zones. If the stem describes active bleeding from a neck wound, the answer is OR, not CTA.


## Slide 27: Retroperitoneal Zones at Laparotomy: 1 Explore, 2 Selective, 3 Don't

type: Data/Table


Retroperitoneal hematoma management at laparotomy is tested because the decision to explore or observe a hematoma depends on both the zone and the mechanism of injury. This requires a mental matrix that you must memorize. Zone 1 is the central or midline retroperitoneum containing the aorta, inferior vena cava, and pancreas. A hematoma here is explored regardless of mechanism because it suggests major vascular injury that will be fatal without repair. Zone 2 is the lateral retroperitoneum containing the kidneys. In blunt trauma, a Zone 2 hematoma is observed unless it is expanding, because most renal injuries can be managed non-operatively and exploration of a stable perinephric hematoma may convert a non-operative injury into a nephrectomy. In penetrating trauma, Zone 2 hematomas are explored because the wound tract may have injured the kidney, ureter, or colon. Zone 3 is the pelvic retroperitoneum containing the iliac vessels and presacral venous plexus. In blunt trauma, a Zone 3 hematoma is NEVER explored because it almost always represents pelvic fracture bleeding from the presacral venous plexus. Opening this hematoma releases the tamponade effect that is controlling the hemorrhage, resulting in massive uncontrollable venous bleeding and potential exsanguination. The correct management is to close the abdomen and proceed to angioembolization or preperitoneal pelvic packing. In penetrating trauma, Zone 3 hematomas are explored because the mechanism may have injured the iliac vessels, which require direct repair. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf loves Zone 3 blunt — the answer is NEVER explore. If a stem describes opening a pelvic hematoma during laparotomy for blunt trauma, that is the wrong answer. Angioembolization is correct.


## Slide 28: Pelvic Fracture Classification Predicts Hemorrhage Risk and Mortality

type: Data/Table


Pelvic fracture classification by mechanism predicts hemorrhage risk and guides management. The three major types are anterior-posterior compression, lateral compression, and vertical shear. Anterior-posterior compression or open-book fractures result from a direct frontal force, typically a head-on motor vehicle collision or motorcycle crash. The force externally rotates the hemipelves, opening the pelvic ring anteriorly at the pubic symphysis. This diastasis increases pelvic volume, disrupts the posterior sacroiliac ligaments in severe cases, and tears pelvic venous plexuses. Hemorrhage risk is moderate to high, and a pelvic binder is particularly effective because it closes the open book and reduces pelvic volume. Lateral compression fractures are the most common type, resulting from a side-impact collision. The force internally rotates the hemipelvis, actually reducing pelvic volume. These fractures have the lowest mortality because the internal rotation compresses rather than disrupts the venous plexus. However, associated injuries to the bladder and urethra can occur. Vertical shear fractures result from falls from height and cause vertical displacement of one hemipelvis relative to the other. These are the most unstable fracture pattern, with complete disruption of both anterior and posterior pelvic ring ligaments. Mortality is highest because the massive ligamentous disruption opens multiple bleeding sources simultaneously, and the instability makes tamponade difficult to achieve. Management escalation for all unstable pelvic fractures begins with a pelvic binder, followed by FAST to determine intra-abdominal hemorrhage, then angioembolization or preperitoneal packing for continued pelvic bleeding. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem describes a fall from height with hemodynamic instability and pelvic fracture, think vertical shear — highest mortality. When it describes a side-impact MVC, think lateral compression — most common but lowest mortality.


## Slide 29: Pelvic Fracture Algorithm: Binder First, FAST Second, Then Decide

type: Content


The pelvic fracture management algorithm is a critical decision tree tested on the shelf because it requires prioritizing competing hemorrhage sources. The algorithm begins with recognition: any hemodynamically unstable patient with a pelvic fracture gets a pelvic binder applied at the level of the greater trochanters. This is done in the primary survey as part of circulation assessment. The binder reduces pelvic volume, approximates fracture fragments, and promotes tamponade of venous bleeding. Next, perform a FAST exam to evaluate for intra-abdominal free fluid. If the FAST is positive, the patient goes to the operating room for exploratory laparotomy — NOT for pelvic packing. This is because intra-abdominal hemorrhage from a ruptured spleen or liver is arterial and will not stop with pelvic stabilization alone. At laparotomy, if a Zone 3 pelvic hematoma is encountered, it must NOT be explored because opening it releases the tamponade effect and causes uncontrollable hemorrhage from the presacral venous plexus. If the FAST is negative but the patient remains hemodynamically unstable despite binder and resuscitation, the bleeding source is the pelvis itself. The next step is angioembolization to identify and embolize arterial bleeders, or preperitoneal pelvic packing in institutions where interventional radiology is not immediately available. The key decision point is that FAST determines whether the patient needs laparotomy (positive FAST) or angioembolization (negative FAST). The pelvic binder is applied regardless and stays on throughout. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf will present an unstable patient with a pelvic fracture and positive FAST and ask the next step. The answer is laparotomy, not pelvic angioembolization. The positive FAST tells you there is a competing abdominal hemorrhage source that takes priority.


## Slide 30: Blood at Meatus Means Retrograde Urethrogram Before Foley — Always

type: Content


Urethral and bladder injuries are commonly associated with pelvic fractures and are tested on the shelf because the management depends on recognizing the injury BEFORE performing an intervention that could worsen it. The three classic signs of urethral injury are blood at the urethral meatus, a high-riding prostate on digital rectal exam, and scrotal or perineal hematoma. When any of these signs are present, a retrograde urethrogram must be performed BEFORE attempting Foley catheter insertion. Blindly passing a Foley catheter through a disrupted urethra can convert a partial tear into a complete transection and create a false passage. The retrograde urethrogram is performed by injecting contrast through the urethral meatus under fluoroscopy to visualize the integrity of the urethra. If the urethra is intact, a Foley can then be safely placed. If disrupted, a suprapubic catheter is placed instead. Bladder injuries from pelvic fractures are classified as either intraperitoneal or extraperitoneal, and the distinction determines management. Intraperitoneal bladder rupture occurs when the dome of a full bladder ruptures, spilling urine into the peritoneal cavity. This requires operative repair because urine in the peritoneum causes chemical peritonitis and sepsis. Extraperitoneal bladder rupture occurs when the fracture fragments lacerate the bladder base. This is managed with Foley catheter drainage alone because most extraperitoneal injuries heal spontaneously within two to three weeks with continuous bladder decompression. A follow-up cystogram confirms healing before the Foley is removed. > **Shelf Tip:** Blood at meatus is a red flag that appears in shelf stems to test whether you know to get a RUG before placing a Foley. If the stem mentions blood at the meatus and the answer choices include Foley placement, that is the wrong answer.


## Slide 31: Compartment Syndrome Has a 6-Hour Window — Pain with Passive Stretch Is the Earliest Sign

type: Content


Compartment syndrome is a time-critical surgical emergency tested on every surgery shelf exam. The mechanism is straightforward: increased pressure within a closed fascial compartment compromises perfusion to the muscles and nerves within that compartment, leading to ischemia and eventually irreversible necrosis. The most common scenario is a tibial shaft fracture affecting the anterior compartment of the lower leg, but it can occur in any fascial compartment including the forearm, thigh, and hand. The clinical findings follow the classic 6 Ps mnemonic, but the shelf specifically tests which findings are early and which are late. Pain out of proportion to the injury is the first symptom, and pain with passive stretch of the muscles in the affected compartment is the earliest and most reliable physical exam finding. For the anterior compartment of the leg, this means passive plantar flexion of the toes causes severe pain. Paresthesias follow as nerve ischemia develops. Paralysis indicates motor nerve death and is a late finding. Pallor and pulselessness are very late findings that indicate vascular compromise — if you wait for absent pulses, irreversible damage has already occurred. The diagnostic threshold is compartment pressure greater than 30 mmHg absolute, or a delta pressure (diastolic minus compartment pressure) less than 30 mmHg. However, the diagnosis is clinical, and fasciotomy should not be delayed for pressure measurements when the clinical picture is clear. Treatment is emergent fasciotomy of all four compartments of the lower leg: anterior, lateral, superficial posterior, and deep posterior. The time window is approximately 6 hours — beyond this, irreversible muscle necrosis (Volkmann contracture) and nerve damage occur. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf will present a tibial fracture with severe pain and ask for the diagnosis or next step. Do NOT be distracted by answer choices checking pulses or ordering imaging. Pain with passive stretch = fasciotomy.


## Slide 32: Gustilo Classification Grades Open Fractures and Drives Antibiotic Selection

type: Data/Table


The Gustilo classification for open fractures is tested because it determines antibiotic selection and predicts outcome, particularly amputation risk. Open fractures are classified by wound size, degree of contamination, soft tissue coverage, and presence of vascular injury. Type I fractures have a wound less than 1 cm with minimal contamination and are treated with cefazolin alone, targeting gram-positive skin flora. Type II fractures have wounds between 1 and 10 cm with moderate soft tissue damage, also treated with cefazolin. Type III fractures are subdivided into three categories. Type IIIA has wounds greater than 10 cm but adequate soft tissue coverage for closure — these receive cefazolin plus an aminoglycoside (typically gentamicin) because the larger wound increases gram-negative contamination risk. Type IIIB also has wounds greater than 10 cm but with inadequate soft tissue coverage, requiring flap coverage in addition to dual antibiotics. Type IIIC is defined by vascular injury requiring repair, regardless of wound size, and carries the highest amputation rate. Several management principles apply to all open fractures. Tetanus prophylaxis is administered based on immunization status. Antibiotics should be given within 1 hour of presentation — delay in antibiotic administration significantly increases infection rates. Operative debridement and irrigation should occur within 6 to 24 hours. The fracture should be stabilized, typically with external fixation initially for severely contaminated or unstable wounds. The key shelf teaching point is the transition from Type II to Type IIIA: this is where gram-negative coverage (aminoglycoside) is added. Farm injuries or those with significant soil contamination may also receive penicillin for Clostridium coverage. > **Shelf Tip:** The shelf tests Gustilo classification by describing an open fracture and asking which antibiotics. The decision point is wound size greater than 10 cm — that is when you add the aminoglycoside. If vascular injury is present, it is always IIIC regardless of wound size.


## Slide 33: Fat Embolism Presents 24-72 Hours Post-Fracture with a Classic Triad

type: Content


Fat embolism syndrome is tested on the shelf because it has a distinctive clinical presentation with a specific time course that differentiates it from other post-traumatic complications. The syndrome occurs 24 to 72 hours after long bone or pelvic fractures, which is a critical timing detail. Fat globules from the bone marrow enter the venous circulation through disrupted medullary vessels and travel to the pulmonary vasculature. In the lungs, they cause mechanical obstruction and inflammatory injury, producing respiratory distress. A proportion of fat globules either pass through the pulmonary capillary bed or cross a patent foramen ovale to enter the systemic circulation, where they can lodge in cerebral and dermal vessels. The classic triad is respiratory distress (dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxia from pulmonary fat embolization), neurologic changes (confusion, agitation, altered mental status from cerebral fat embolization), and petechial rash in a characteristic distribution on the chest, axillae, and conjunctivae. The petechial rash is pathognomonic and results from fat globule obstruction of dermal capillaries with platelet consumption. It is present in approximately 50-60% of cases and is the most specific finding. The diagnosis is clinical — there is no definitive laboratory test. Bronchoalveolar lavage showing fat-laden macrophages and retinal examination showing fat emboli can support the diagnosis. Treatment is supportive with supplemental oxygen and mechanical ventilation as needed. There is no specific pharmacologic therapy. Prevention through early fixation of long bone fractures (within 24 hours) significantly reduces the incidence of fat embolism syndrome by stabilizing the fracture and reducing ongoing marrow fat release into the circulation. > **Shelf Tip:** The timing is the key to the diagnosis. When a stem describes a patient who was stable after a femur fracture and develops confusion plus dyspnea plus a rash 2 days later, the answer is fat embolism syndrome. The 24-72 hour delay is what distinguishes this from pulmonary embolism or pneumonia.


## Slide 34: Damage Control Surgery Saves Lives by Treating Physiology Before Anatomy

type: Content


Damage control surgery is a paradigm shift in trauma management that prioritizes correcting physiologic derangement over completing anatomic repair. The concept recognizes that the lethal triad of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy creates a self-reinforcing cycle that kills patients on the operating table during prolonged definitive procedures. The trigger for damage control is the presence of any two elements of the lethal triad: core temperature below 35 degrees Celsius, pH below 7.2, and clinical coagulopathy (typically INR greater than 1.5 or non-surgical bleeding). When these criteria are met, attempting a definitive repair is more dangerous than an abbreviated operation. Phase one occurs in the operating room and should take less than 60 minutes. The goals are limited: control hemorrhage (packing, ligation, shunting) and control contamination (stapling off injured bowel, diverting enteric contents). The abdomen is temporarily closed, typically with a negative pressure wound therapy system or Bogota bag. No anastomoses, no complex repairs. Phase two occurs in the ICU and focuses on breaking the lethal triad: active rewarming to normalize temperature, blood product transfusion and factor correction to reverse coagulopathy, and volume resuscitation plus vasopressor support to correct acidosis. This phase typically takes 24 to 72 hours until the patient's physiology normalizes. Phase three returns to the operating room for definitive repair once the lethal triad has been corrected: bowel anastomosis, complex vascular repair, and definitive abdominal closure. The key teaching point is that damage control is a deliberate strategic decision, not a failure of operative technique. > **Shelf Tip:** When a stem describes a trauma patient in the OR with hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy, the answer is damage control (pack and close), not definitive repair. The shelf tests whether you recognize the lethal triad as the trigger.


## Slide 35: 30M GSW abdomen. OR: liver laceration + small bowel injuries. Temp 34.2, pH 7.18, INR 2.1. Best approach?

type: MCQ


This question presents the classic damage control surgery scenario with all three elements of the lethal triad present: hypothermia (34.2 degrees Celsius, below 35), acidosis (pH 7.18, below 7.2), and coagulopathy (INR 2.1, well above normal). The patient has both a liver laceration and small bowel injuries from a gunshot wound. Option A, complete liver repair and bowel anastomosis, is the definitive repair that would be appropriate if the patient were physiologically normal. However, performing a complex hepatic repair and bowel anastomosis in the setting of the lethal triad will result in worsening coagulopathy from ongoing blood loss during the prolonged procedure, failed anastomosis from tissue edema and poor perfusion, and likely death on the table. Option B is correct: pack the liver to control hemorrhage (liver packing is one of the most effective damage control techniques), resect the injured bowel segments and staple the ends (controlling contamination without creating an anastomosis that will fail), and temporarily close the abdomen. The patient then goes to the ICU for phase two resuscitation. Option C is wrong because leaving bowel injuries unaddressed allows ongoing fecal contamination of the peritoneum, which will cause sepsis and death. Damage control controls BOTH hemorrhage and contamination. Option D is nonsensical — the patient has ongoing intra-abdominal injuries that require operative management. The teaching point is that damage control addresses all sources of hemorrhage and contamination while avoiding time-consuming definitive repairs. > **Shelf Tip:** Count the lethal triad elements in the stem. ## Slide 36: Damage Control: All Three Elements of Lethal Triad Present — Abbreviated Surgery Saves Lives

type: Content


The correct answer is B, pack the liver, resect and staple the bowel, and perform temporary abdominal closure. This is textbook damage control surgery. Let us review why each step is necessary and why the other options are wrong. Packing the liver controls hemorrhage without requiring a complex hepatic repair. Perihepatic packing is remarkably effective for controlling venous bleeding from hepatic lacerations, and the packs provide tamponade while the patient resuscitates in the ICU. The packs are removed during the phase three definitive operation. Resecting and stapling the injured bowel segments controls contamination by preventing ongoing fecal spillage into the peritoneum. The bowel ends are stapled shut — no anastomosis is created because anastomoses made in the setting of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy have an extremely high leak rate due to tissue edema and impaired healing. The anastomosis is deferred to phase three when the patient's physiology has normalized. Temporary abdominal closure allows for bowel edema (which is inevitable after massive resuscitation) without causing abdominal compartment syndrome. A negative pressure wound therapy system or Bogota bag is applied, and the abdomen is formally closed during a subsequent operation. The lethal triad parameters in this stem are all clearly abnormal: temperature 34.2 (hypothermia), pH 7.18 (severe metabolic acidosis from hemorrhagic shock), and INR 2.1 (coagulopathy from both consumption and dilution). Attempting definitive repair with these numbers would result in a prolonged operation during which the patient's physiology spirals further, ultimately leading to death on the table or shortly after in the ICU.


## Slide 37: Three Populations Require Modified Trauma Management — Recognize the Patterns

type: Data/Table


Special populations modify standard trauma algorithms in ways that the shelf specifically tests. For pediatric trauma, the most important concept is that children have a highly compliant chest wall that absorbs energy without fracturing. Therefore, rib fractures in a child indicate extremely high-force injury and should raise suspicion for non-accidental trauma (child abuse). The combination of retinal hemorrhages and subdural hematomas is pathognomonic for shaken baby syndrome. Other abuse indicators include metaphyseal corner fractures, spiral fractures of long bones in pre-ambulatory children, and multiple fractures at different stages of healing. Fluid resuscitation uses 20 mL per kg boluses of lactated Ringer's. For geriatric trauma, falls are the number one mechanism, not motor vehicle collisions. The critical physiologic differences are decreased physiologic reserve (patients decompensate rapidly), medication effects (beta-blockers and pacemakers mask tachycardia, making hemorrhage recognition difficult), and anticoagulation (increased bleeding risk, lower threshold for CT head after any fall). Each additional rib fracture in patients over 65 increases mortality by approximately 19%. Chronic subdural hematoma is a classic geriatric presentation — the elderly patient on anticoagulants who falls and presents weeks later with progressive confusion. For pregnant trauma patients, the cardinal rule is that the best treatment for the fetus is treating the mother. After 20 weeks of gestation, the patient should be positioned in the left lateral decubitus position to avoid IVC compression by the gravid uterus. Pregnant patients can lose 30-35% of their blood volume before showing signs of shock due to their expanded blood volume. The Kleihauer-Betke test detects fetal red blood cells in maternal circulation and guides Rh immunoglobulin dosing. Perimortem cesarean section should be performed within 4-5 minutes of maternal cardiac arrest if the fetus is greater than 24 weeks gestational age. > **Shelf Tip:** Pediatric rib fracture = abuse. Elderly fall with normal HR on beta-blockers = occult hemorrhage. Pregnant with normal vitals = may be losing significant volume. Each population has a specific trap the shelf uses.

This question presents the classic non-accidental trauma (child abuse) scenario. The stem provides several red flags. ## Slide 39: Non-Accidental Trauma — Bilateral Subdurals + Retinal Hemorrhages = Abuse Until Proven Otherwise

type: Content


The correct answer is C, non-accidental trauma. This question tests the ability to recognize the pattern of abusive head trauma, formerly called shaken baby syndrome, which is one of the most important diagnoses in pediatric trauma. The key elements that confirm the diagnosis are the inconsistent history (a low-energy mechanism cannot produce the described injuries), bilateral subdural hematomas (the hallmark of acceleration-deceleration injury from shaking), and retinal hemorrhages (present in over 80% of abusive head trauma cases and highly specific for this diagnosis).
