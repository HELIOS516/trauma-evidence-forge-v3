## ATLS 11th Edition Restructured the Primary Survey: X Now Comes Before A

- ATLS = Advanced Trauma Life Support (ACS course since 1980, 80+ countries)
- **X** = eXsanguination (hemorrhage control)
- **A** = Airway (with cervical spine protection)
- **B** = Breathing (ventilation and chest injuries)
- **C** = Circulation (shock assessment, IV access, FAST)
- **D** = Disability (neurologic status, GCS)
- **E** = Exposure (full exam, hypothermia prevention)
- **[KEY STAT: ATLS 11th Edition (2025) formally added "X" -- the biggest change in 45 years]**

> **Bottom Line:** The order is the point -- treat first what kills first.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Questions often test whether you know the correct sequence. X before A before B before C.

**Sources:** [18]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
ATLS stands for Advanced Trauma Life Support. It is a course developed by the American College of Surgeons, first published in 1980, and now taught in over 80 countries to more than one million providers. The idea was revolutionary: create a standardized, reproducible approach to the injured patient so that every physician, whether in a rural ED or a Level 1 trauma center, follows the same sequence. That sequence is the primary survey. The 11th edition, released in July 2025, made the most significant revision in the course's 45-year history: it formally added the X step. Let me spell out each letter. X stands for eXsanguination. Before you do anything else, look for and control life-threatening external hemorrhage. Apply a tourniquet, pack a wound, put on a pelvic binder. This takes seconds. A stands for Airway with cervical spine protection. Is the airway open? Is the patient talking? B stands for Breathing. You are looking for five immediately life-threatening chest injuries. C stands for Circulation. Assess for hemorrhagic shock, obtain IV access, start resuscitation, perform the FAST exam. FAST stands for Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma -- a bedside ultrasound looking for internal bleeding. D stands for Disability -- the rapid neurologic exam using the Glasgow Coma Scale, or GCS. E stands for Exposure -- fully undress the patient to find all injuries, then actively prevent hypothermia. The order is not arbitrary. It reflects the speed at which each problem kills. Hemorrhage kills in minutes. Airway obstruction kills in minutes. The primary survey addresses threats in order of lethality.
-->

## Exsanguination Is the Leading Cause of Preventable Trauma Death

- Hemorrhage causes 30-40% of trauma mortality
- 90.9% of survivable battlefield deaths from bleeding
- Tourniquet application takes 15-30 seconds
- Trauma = leading cause of death ages 1-44 in the US
- **[KEY STAT: 90.9% of preventable deaths = hemorrhage (Eastridge 2012)]**

> **Bottom Line:** The X in xABCDE exists because hemorrhage kills fastest.

> **Shelf exam tip:** "Leading cause of preventable trauma death" = hemorrhage. One of the most commonly tested facts.

**Sources:** [11][18]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
Why does the X come first? Because the data are overwhelming. Trauma is the leading cause of death for ages 1 to 44 in the United States, with approximately 225,000 unintentional injury deaths per year. Among those deaths, hemorrhage accounts for 30 to 40 percent of all trauma mortality and is the leading cause of potentially preventable death. The landmark study is from Eastridge and colleagues, published in the Journal of Trauma and Acute Care Surgery in 2012. They analyzed 4,596 combat fatalities from Iraq and Afghanistan between 2001 and 2011 using the Joint Theater Trauma Registry. They found that 24.3 percent of deaths were potentially survivable -- meaning the injuries were not inherently fatal. Of those potentially survivable deaths, 90.9 percent were caused by hemorrhage. The breakdown: 67.3 percent from truncal hemorrhage, which is non-compressible; 19.2 percent from junctional hemorrhage at the groin, axilla, or neck; and 13.5 percent from extremity hemorrhage, which is compressible with a tourniquet. That military data directly drove the civilian paradigm shift. If hemorrhage kills more trauma patients than anything else, and many of those deaths are preventable with rapid intervention, then the first step in the primary survey must be hemorrhage control. A tourniquet takes 15 to 30 seconds to apply. RSI, which stands for Rapid Sequence Intubation, the standard method for emergency intubation, takes 3 to 5 minutes. A patient bleeding 300 milliliters per minute from a femoral artery can lose more than a liter during intubation setup.

## Massive External Hemorrhage Control Precedes Airway Management

- Tourniquet for extremity hemorrhage (15-30 seconds to apply)
- Direct pressure + hemostatic gauze for junctional wounds
- Pelvic binder at greater trochanters for pelvic fracture
- Activate MTP if ABC score >=2 or Shock Index >1.0
- **[KEY STAT: Tourniquet = 15-30 sec; RSI = 3-5 min -- the time gap kills]**

> **Bottom Line:** In exsanguination, stop the bleeding before managing the airway.

**Sources:** [5][11][18]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
What does the X step actually look like in practice? Three interventions, and you should know all three. First, tourniquets for extremity hemorrhage. If a patient has an arterial bleed from an arm or leg, a commercial tourniquet such as the Combat Application Tourniquet, or CAT, is applied as proximal as possible, meaning as high up on the limb as you can. Tighten until the bleeding stops. Note the time. This was originally a military intervention now validated in the civilian setting. Second, direct pressure and wound packing for junctional wounds. Junctional means the groin, the axilla, and the neck -- areas where a tourniquet cannot be applied because there is no single bone to compress against. For these wounds, you pack hemostatic gauze, such as Combat Gauze impregnated with kaolin, directly into the wound and hold sustained pressure for at least 3 minutes. Third, pelvic binders. Our patient has a deformed pelvis. An unstable pelvic ring fracture can result in several liters of hemorrhage into the retroperitoneum. A circumferential pelvic binder placed at the level of the greater trochanters reduces pelvic volume, tamponades venous bleeding, and stabilizes bony fragments. This happens in the trauma bay before anything else. Think about our patient: he has a GCS of 8, but he also has an unstable pelvis and a distended abdomen. We apply the pelvic binder and call for MTP. MTP stands for Massive Transfusion Protocol -- the blood bank sends a cooler of balanced blood products. Then we assess the airway. The sequence matters because treating a patent airway before stopping the hemorrhage wastes the minutes that determine survival.

## The Airway Algorithm Moves From Basic Maneuvers to RSI to Surgical Airway

- Jaw thrust + suction + remove debris first
- RSI = Rapid Sequence Intubation (sedative + paralytic)
- GCS <=8: strong indication for definitive airway
- Surgical cricothyrotomy if RSI fails or anatomy precludes intubation
- **[KEY STAT: GCS <=8 is a strong indication, but clinical context determines the decision]**

> **Bottom Line:** Assess, protect, then secure the airway -- always have a surgical backup plan.

> **Shelf exam tip:** RSI drugs = sedative (ketamine or etomidate) + paralytic (succinylcholine or rocuronium). Know them.

**Sources:** [10][15][16]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
Once hemorrhage control is addressed, we move to A for Airway with cervical spine protection. The approach is stepwise. Step one: assessment. Is the patient talking? If yes, the airway is patent, meaning open and clear. If no, look for obstruction from blood, vomitus, broken teeth, or soft tissue swelling. Listen for stridor, a high-pitched sound indicating narrowing, gurgling from fluid, or snoring from tongue obstruction. Step two: basic maneuvers. In trauma, use the jaw thrust, not the head-tilt chin-lift, because you must protect the cervical spine in blunt trauma. The jaw thrust pulls the mandible forward and lifts the tongue. Suction secretions. Remove visible foreign bodies. Step three: if basic maneuvers fail, place a supraglottic airway as a bridge device. Step four: definitive airway. In trauma, this means RSI, or Rapid Sequence Intubation. RSI involves giving a sedative, typically ketamine or etomidate, followed immediately by a neuromuscular blocking agent, classically succinylcholine or rocuronium, then intubating with an endotracheal tube. GCS of 8 or less is the classic indication. But the 2021 study by Hatchimonji using the National Trauma Data Bank showed that rigid application of the GCS-8 rule, without considering clinical context, may increase mortality in some patients. A GCS of 7 from alcohol intoxication with intact gag reflex is different from a GCS of 7 from a subdural hematoma with active vomiting. Use GCS as one data point, not the only data point. Critical teaching: always have a surgical airway plan before attempting laryngoscopy. If RSI fails or anatomy precludes intubation, such as massive facial trauma, you proceed to surgical cricothyrotomy -- cutting through the cricothyroid membrane to place an airway directly into the trachea.
-->

<!-- Speaker Notes:

## Tension Pneumothorax Is a Clinical Diagnosis That Requires Immediate Decompression

- Hypotension + absent breath sounds = decompress now
- Never delay for CXR (chest X-ray) or any imaging
- Needle decompression: 2nd ICS MCL or 5th ICS AAL
- Follow with tube thoracostomy (chest tube)
- **[KEY STAT: Clinical diagnosis -- do NOT wait for imaging]**

> **Bottom Line:** Suspected tension pneumothorax is treated, not imaged.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Tension PTX = clinical diagnosis + immediate needle decompression. One of the most tested emergency procedures.

**Sources:** [17]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
Moving to B for Breathing. The B step focuses on identifying and treating immediately life-threatening chest injuries. The most time-critical is tension pneumothorax. Let me explain the pathophysiology. Normally, the pleural space between the lung and the chest wall has negative pressure that keeps the lung inflated. In a tension pneumothorax, air enters the pleural space through a lung injury but cannot escape, creating a one-way valve. With each breath, more air accumulates. The ipsilateral lung collapses. The mediastinum, containing the heart and great vessels, shifts to the opposite side, compressing the other lung. Most critically, the increased intrathoracic pressure impairs venous return to the right heart, producing obstructive shock. The diagnosis is clinical. The most reliable signs are hypotension combined with unilateral absent breath sounds. Other signs include distended neck veins, or JVD, which stands for jugular venous distension, though these may be absent in the hypovolemic patient. Tracheal deviation is a late and unreliable finding. If you suspect tension pneumothorax, you decompress immediately. Needle decompression uses a 14-gauge angiocatheter at least 8 centimeters long at the 2nd intercostal space midclavicular line, or the 4th or 5th intercostal space anterior axillary line. The 5th ICS AAL is increasingly preferred because the chest wall is thinner there. Definitive treatment is a tube thoracostomy, meaning a chest tube. The critical error, and the one the shelf will test, is waiting for a chest X-ray. The patient will arrest while you wait for the film.

## Five Immediately Life-Threatening Chest Injuries Must Be Found in the B Step

- 1. Tension pneumothorax -- needle decompress immediately
- 2. Open pneumothorax -- 3-sided occlusive dressing
- 3. Massive hemothorax -- chest tube; >1,500 mL or >200 mL/hr = OR
- 4. Flail chest -- positive pressure ventilation, pain control
- 5. Cardiac tamponade -- Beck's triad; pericardiocentesis or thoracotomy
- **[KEY STAT: 5 lethal chest injuries -- all diagnosed clinically in the primary survey]**

> **Bottom Line:** Identify and treat all five before moving to the circulation assessment.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Memorize all five. Beck's triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds) = tamponade.

**Sources:** [17][18]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
You must know these five injuries cold. They are among the highest-yield content on the surgery shelf and on USMLE Step 2. Number one: tension pneumothorax, which we just covered in detail. Number two: open pneumothorax, also called a sucking chest wound. This is a wound in the chest wall large enough, typically two-thirds the diameter of the trachea, that air preferentially enters the pleural space through the wound rather than through the trachea. Treatment is a three-sided occlusive dressing, which creates a flutter valve: air can exit through the open side during exhalation but the dressing seals during inhalation, preventing air entry. A chest tube is then placed at a site separate from the wound. Number three: massive hemothorax. Defined as more than 1,500 milliliters of blood immediately drained after chest tube insertion, or ongoing output of more than 200 milliliters per hour. Initial treatment is a 28- to 32-French chest tube. If output exceeds those thresholds, the patient needs an operation. Number four: flail chest with pulmonary contusion. A flail segment occurs when three or more consecutive ribs are fractured in two or more places, creating paradoxical chest wall motion. The underlying pulmonary contusion causes the hypoxia. Treatment is positive pressure ventilation and aggressive pain control. Number five: cardiac tamponade. Blood fills the pericardial sac and compresses the heart, preventing it from filling. Beck's triad is the classic presentation: hypotension, JVD, which stands for jugular venous distension, and muffled heart sounds. Most common with penetrating trauma. Treatment is pericardiocentesis or emergency thoracotomy.

## "Blood on the Floor and Four More" Localizes the Five Sources of Hemorrhage

- 5 places to bleed: chest, abdomen, pelvis, long bones, external
- FAST (Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma) for abdominal free fluid
- CXR for hemothorax; pelvic XR for fracture
- External hemorrhage should already be controlled in X step
- **[KEY STAT: 5 hemorrhage sources -- chest, abdomen, pelvis, long bones, external]**

> **Bottom Line:** Identify the bleeding source to direct surgical or interventional control.

**Sources:** [1][18]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
Now we move to C for Circulation. The first task is to identify where the patient is bleeding. The classic mnemonic is "blood on the floor and four more." That gives you five compartments. First, external, meaning blood you can see on the floor, on the sheets, and on the patient. This should already be controlled in the X step. Second, the chest. A hemothorax, which is blood in the pleural space, can hold 2 to 3 liters on each side. You assess with a chest X-ray. Third, the abdomen. The peritoneal cavity can hold the patient's entire blood volume. You assess with the FAST exam. FAST stands for Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma. It is a bedside ultrasound performed in the trauma bay that looks at four windows: the right upper quadrant around the liver and kidney, a space called Morrison's pouch; the left upper quadrant around the spleen and kidney; the suprapubic view looking for fluid around the bladder; and the subxiphoid view looking at the heart for pericardial fluid. Fourth, the pelvis. An unstable pelvic fracture can result in massive retroperitoneal hemorrhage. You assess with a pelvic X-ray and clinical exam. Fifth, the long bones, specifically the femurs. A single femur fracture can result in 1 to 1.5 liters of blood loss. In our patient, the distended abdomen and deformed pelvis point to at least two hemorrhage sources. Simultaneously, you are obtaining IV access -- two large-bore peripheral IVs, meaning 16-gauge or larger, or an intraosseous line if peripheral access fails -- and initiating resuscitation.

## Class III and IV Hemorrhagic Shock Demand Immediate Blood Product Resuscitation

| Parameter | Class I | Class II | Class III | Class IV |
|-----------|---------|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Blood loss** | <750 mL (<15%) | 750-1,500 mL (15-30%) | 1,500-2,000 mL (30-40%) | >2,000 mL (>40%) |
| **Heart rate** | <100 | 100-120 | **120-140** | **>140** |
| **Blood pressure** | Normal | Normal | **Decreased** | **Markedly decreased** |
| **Respiratory rate** | 14-20 | 20-30 | 30-40 | >35 |
| **Urine output** | >30 mL/h | 20-30 mL/h | 5-15 mL/h | Negligible |
| **GCS** | 15 | 14-15 | 12-14 | <12 |
| **Treatment** | Monitor | Possible transfusion | **Blood products** | **MTP activate; emergent OR** |

> **Bottom Line:** Our patient (HR 130, BP 80/50, GCS 8) fits Class III-IV -- activate MTP now.

> **Shelf exam tip:** This table is one of the most tested on the surgery shelf. Know the class boundaries and treatments cold.

**Sources:** [18]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
This table is absolutely critical for your shelf exam. It is the ATLS hemorrhagic shock classification, dividing shock into four classes based on estimated blood loss and physiologic response. Class I is less than 750 milliliters, under 15 percent of blood volume, which is about 70 mL per kg in a 70-kg adult, so total blood volume is roughly 5 liters. The patient looks essentially normal. Heart rate under 100, normal blood pressure. Treatment is monitoring. Class II is 750 to 1,500 milliliters, 15 to 30 percent. Heart rate rises to 100 to 120, but blood pressure is still normal. The pulse pressure, the difference between systolic and diastolic, narrows. Treatment may include transfusion. Class III is where things get dangerous. Blood loss of 1,500 to 2,000 milliliters, 30 to 40 percent. Heart rate 120 to 140, blood pressure drops, respiratory rate increases, urine output falls, GCS drops. This patient needs blood products. Class IV is greater than 2,000 milliliters, over 40 percent. Heart rate above 140, profoundly hypotensive, minimal urine output, obtunded. This is massive transfusion territory and likely needs emergent surgery. A critical pearl: young, healthy adults compensate extremely well. Their blood pressure may remain normal until they have lost 30 to 40 percent of their blood volume. By the time blood pressure drops, they are already in Class III. Do not rely on blood pressure alone. Use heart rate, mental status, and the Shock Index. Our patient: HR 130, BP 80/50, GCS 8. He is Class III to IV and needs MTP now.

## Shock Index and ABC Score Trigger MTP Activation at the Bedside

- Shock Index (SI) = HR / SBP; normal 0.5-0.7
- SI >1.0 = 4-fold increase in mortality risk

## 1:1:1 Balanced Resuscitation Replaced Crystalloid-First Dogma in Hemorrhagic Shock

- PROPPR trial (2015): 1:1:1 pRBC:plasma:platelets ratio
- 1:1:1 achieved hemostasis in 86% vs 78% at 24h (p=0.006)
- Limit crystalloid (NS, LR) to <1 liter before blood products
- Crystalloid worsens the lethal triad (hypothermia + acidosis + coagulopathy)
- **[KEY STAT: PROPPR: 1:1:1 hemostasis 86% vs 78% at 24h (p=0.006, n=680)]**

> **Bottom Line:** Blood products, not crystalloid, are the resuscitation fluid of choice in hemorrhagic shock.

> **Shelf exam tip:** "2 liters normal saline" is almost always wrong in hemorrhagic shock. Choose blood products.

**Sources:** [5][12][13]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
Once MTP is activated, what do you give? The landmark trial is PROPPR, which stands for Pragmatic, Randomized Optimal Platelet and Plasma Ratios, published in JAMA in 2015. It was a multicenter randomized controlled trial across 12 US Level 1 trauma centers enrolling 680 patients with severe trauma and hemorrhage. Patients were randomized to receive blood products in a 1:1:1 ratio, meaning one unit of plasma and one unit of platelets for every unit of packed red blood cells, or pRBC, versus a 1:1:2 ratio. The primary finding: 1:1:1 achieved hemostasis at 24 hours in 86 percent versus 78 percent in the 1:1:2 group, with a p-value of 0.006. There was also a trend toward lower 24-hour mortality. The practical implication: when MTP is activated, the blood bank sends products in a 1:1:1 ratio. What you do not give in large volumes is crystalloid, meaning normal saline, abbreviated NS, or lactated Ringer's, abbreviated LR. The old teaching was to start with 2 liters of crystalloid. That approach has been abandoned in hemorrhagic shock because crystalloid dilutes clotting factors, drops body temperature, and worsens acidosis. This feeds the lethal triad: hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy. EAST guidelines and the ACS recommend limiting crystalloid to less than 1 liter before blood products are available. For the shelf: if a hemorrhagic shock question offers "2 liters normal saline," it is almost certainly the wrong answer.

## TXA Must Be Given Within 3 Hours of Injury -- After That, It Causes Harm

- TXA = tranexamic acid (antifibrinolytic drug)
- CRASH-2 (2010): n=20,211, largest trauma RCT ever conducted
- <1 hour: 32% reduction in hemorrhagic death (RR 0.68)
- >3 hours: 44% INCREASE in death (RR 1.44, 95% CI 1.12-1.84)
- Dose: 1g IV bolus over 10 min, then 1g IV over 8 hours
- **[KEY STAT: TXA >3h post-injury INCREASES mortality (RR 1.44)]**

> **Bottom Line:** Give TXA early or not at all -- the 3-hour window is a hard cutoff.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Know CRASH-2 by name, the 3-hour window, the dose, and that late TXA causes harm. Extremely high yield.

**Sources:** [3][4]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
TXA stands for tranexamic acid. It is an antifibrinolytic drug, meaning it prevents the breakdown of blood clots. In trauma, the body's fibrinolytic system can become overactive and worsen hemorrhage. TXA inhibits this process. The evidence comes from CRASH-2, published in the Lancet in 2010. It is the largest randomized controlled trial in trauma history: 20,211 patients across 274 hospitals in 40 countries. Patients with significant hemorrhage were randomized to TXA versus placebo. The critical finding came from the 2011 timing subgroup analysis published by Roberts and colleagues. TXA benefit is entirely time-dependent. Given within 1 hour of injury: hemorrhagic death reduced by 32 percent, with a relative risk of 0.68, meaning 32 percent fewer hemorrhagic deaths compared to placebo. Given between 1 and 3 hours: 21 percent reduction, RR 0.79. Given after 3 hours: TXA actually increased hemorrhagic death by 44 percent, RR 1.44, with a 95 percent confidence interval of 1.12 to 1.84. A confidence interval that does not cross 1.0 means the result is statistically significant. Late TXA is harmful. The biological explanation is that after 3 hours, the fibrinolytic surge has largely resolved, and giving an antifibrinolytic promotes pathologic clotting. The dose is 1 gram IV over 10 minutes as a loading dose, then 1 gram infused over 8 hours. Use the time of injury, not arrival time, to calculate the window. If injury was more than 3 hours ago, do not give TXA.

## FAST Answers One Question: Is There Free Fluid in the Unstable Patient?

- FAST = Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma
- 4 windows: RUQ (Morrison's pouch), LUQ, suprapubic, subxiphoid
- Sensitivity 74%, specificity 98% for free fluid
- Positive FAST + hypotension = OR (operating room)
- Negative FAST does NOT exclude injury (26% miss rate)
- **[KEY STAT: Sensitivity only 74% -- negative FAST cannot rule out injury]**

> **Bottom Line:** FAST rules in; it does not rule out -- a negative FAST demands further workup.

> **Shelf exam tip:** FAST is a "rule-in" test, not a "rule-out" test. The 74% sensitivity is frequently tested.

**Sources:** [1]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
The FAST exam is one of the most important bedside tools in trauma. FAST stands for Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma. It is a rapid bedside ultrasound performed during the C step. It looks at four acoustic windows. First, the right upper quadrant, examining Morrison's pouch between the liver and right kidney. This is the most dependent space in the supine patient and where free fluid accumulates first. Second, the left upper quadrant, examining the splenorenal recess between the spleen and left kidney. Third, the suprapubic view, looking for fluid around the bladder. Fourth, the subxiphoid view, looking at the heart for pericardial effusion. Some centers add thoracic views for pneumothorax, called eFAST. The Netherton meta-analysis of 2019 established the key numbers. For intra-abdominal free fluid: sensitivity is 74 percent and specificity is 98 percent. What does this mean? Specificity of 98 percent means if the FAST is positive and shows free fluid, it is almost certainly there. Excellent rule-in test. Sensitivity of 74 percent means about 1 in 4 patients with significant intra-abdominal bleeding will have a negative FAST. That is a 26 percent miss rate. A negative FAST in a patient with concerning mechanism and hemodynamic instability should not reassure you. It should prompt further workup: CT scan if the patient is stable, serial exams, or even operative exploration if the patient is unstable with high clinical suspicion. Think of FAST as a decision support tool, not a standalone diagnostic test.

## GCS Components Plus Pupil Exam Constitute the Rapid Disability Assessment

- GCS = Glasgow Coma Scale: Eye (1-4) + Verbal (1-5) + Motor (1-6) = 3-15
- GCS <=8 = severe TBI (Traumatic Brain Injury)
- Motor component is the best single predictor of outcome
- Pupils: bilateral reactive = reassuring; unilateral fixed = herniation emergency
- Check blood glucose: hypoglycemia mimics TBI
- **[KEY STAT: Always report GCS by components (E/V/M), not just the total -- motor matters most]**

> **Bottom Line:** Report GCS by components, not just the total -- motor score drives prognosis.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Know each GCS component and its scoring range. Motor score is the single best predictor of outcome.

**Sources:** [10][18]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
D is for Disability -- the rapid neurologic assessment. It has two components: the Glasgow Coma Scale and the pupillary exam. Let me break down GCS since you will use it every day. ## Exposure and Hypothermia Prevention Must Begin in the Trauma Bay

- Full exposure: cut all clothing, log roll for posterior exam
- Lethal triad: hypothermia + acidosis + coagulopathy
- Warm OR (>24 C), warm blood products, forced-air blankets
- Hypothermia <35 C independently doubles mortality
- **[KEY STAT: 47.8% mortality when the full lethal triad is present]**

> **Bottom Line:** Undress the patient to find injuries, then actively prevent hypothermia.

**Sources:** [12][13]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
E is for Exposure. Two equally important purposes. First, fully expose the patient to identify all injuries. Cut off all clothing with trauma shears. You cannot treat what you cannot see. Perform a log roll: with cervical spine stabilization, roll the patient onto their side and examine the entire posterior torso, spine, buttocks, and perineum. Injuries hide posteriorly -- stab wounds to the back, posterior scalp lacerations, sacral fractures. Second, immediately prevent hypothermia. The lethal triad, also called the trauma triad of death, is the self-reinforcing cycle of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy. Here is how it works: hemorrhage decreases oxygen delivery, causing anaerobic metabolism and lactic acidosis. Acidosis at pH below 7.1 impairs thrombin generation, the clotting enzyme, by up to 90 percent. Hypothermia below 35 degrees Celsius directly impairs platelet function and coagulation enzyme kinetics. Impaired coagulation worsens hemorrhage, which worsens acidosis, which worsens hypothermia. When all three are present, mortality reaches 47.8 percent. Breaking the cycle requires active warming: warm the trauma bay, use forced-air warming blankets, administer warm blood products through inline fluid warmers, keep the OR temperature above 24 degrees Celsius. Do not wait for hypothermia to develop -- prevention starts the moment the patient arrives. Expose, examine, cover, warm. That is the E step.

## Elderly and TBI Patients Require Modified Shock Assessment

- Elderly (>65): beta-blockers mask tachycardia; SBP <110 may = Class III
- TBI: avoid SBP <90 and SpO2 <90; both independently worsen outcome
- TBI: permissive hypotension is ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED
- Anticoagulated: 4-factor PCC for warfarin; idarucizumab for dabigatran
- **[KEY STAT: Elderly on beta-blockers may have normal HR in Class III shock -- lower your threshold]**

> **Bottom Line:** Standard vital sign thresholds fail in special populations -- adjust your suspicion.

> **Shelf exam tip:** "Permissive hypotension is contraindicated in TBI" is a frequently tested exception.

**Sources:** [18]

<!-- Speaker Notes:
Two special populations where standard parameters can mislead you. First, elderly patients over 65. Many take beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers, which blunt the tachycardic response to hemorrhage. Their heart rate may be 80 in Class III shock, when it should be 130. You cannot rely on heart rate to detect shock in these patients. Additionally, their baseline blood pressure may be higher, so an SBP of 110 may represent a significant drop and correspond to Class III. Always ask what medications the patient takes. Lower your threshold for MTP activation. Second, TBI patients. The brain is exquisitely sensitive to hypotension and hypoxia. An SBP below 90 in TBI independently worsens neurologic outcome. An SpO2 below 90 independently worsens outcome. This means permissive hypotension, a strategy where you deliberately keep blood pressure low to avoid disrupting clots, is absolutely contraindicated in TBI. You must maintain adequate cerebral perfusion even while managing hemorrhage elsewhere. Other populations: anticoagulated patients need reversal agents -- 4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate, or PCC, for warfarin; idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors. Pediatric patients compensate extremely well until they decompensate suddenly and catastrophically. Pregnant patients need left lateral tilt to relieve aortocaval compression from the gravid uterus.
