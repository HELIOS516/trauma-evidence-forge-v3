## xABCDE: Hemorrhage Kills First, So Treat It First

Evan DeCan, MD
Division of Acute Care Surgery | University of Virginia
March 2026

---

## Learning Objectives

1. **Define** the xABCDE framework and explain why hemorrhage control precedes airway management
2. **Apply** the xABCDE sequence to systematically prioritize life-threatening interventions in a trauma patient
3. **Identify** clinical indications for definitive airway management, needle decompression, and MTP (Massive Transfusion Protocol) activation
4. **Classify** hemorrhagic shock by class (I-IV) and recognize when to activate MTP

---

## ATLS 11th Edition Restructured the Primary Survey: X Now Comes Before A

- ATLS = Advanced Trauma Life Support (ACS course since 1980, 80+ countries)
- **X** = eXsanguination (hemorrhage control)
- **A** = Airway (with cervical spine protection)
- **B** = Breathing (ventilation and chest injuries)
- **C** = Circulation (shock assessment, IV access, FAST)
- **D** = Disability (neurologic status, GCS)
- **E** = Exposure (full exam, hypothermia prevention)
- **[KEY STAT: ATLS 11th Edition (2025) formally added "X" -- the biggest change in 45 years]**

> **Bottom Line:** The order is the point -- treat first what kills first.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Questions often test whether you know the correct sequence. X before A before B before C.


**Sources:**
- [18] American 2025

## Exsanguination Is the Leading Cause of Preventable Trauma Death

- Hemorrhage causes 30-40% of trauma mortality
- 90.9% of survivable battlefield deaths from bleeding
- Tourniquet application takes 15-30 seconds
- Trauma = leading cause of death ages 1-44 in the US
- **[KEY STAT: 90.9% of preventable deaths = hemorrhage (Eastridge 2012)]**

> **Bottom Line:** The X in xABCDE exists because hemorrhage kills fastest.

> **Shelf exam tip:** "Leading cause of preventable trauma death" = hemorrhage. One of the most commonly tested facts.


**Sources:**
- [11] [Eastridge 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23192066/)
- [18] American 2025

## Massive External Hemorrhage Control Precedes Airway Management

- Tourniquet for extremity hemorrhage (15-30 seconds to apply)
- Direct pressure + hemostatic gauze for junctional wounds
- Pelvic binder at greater trochanters for pelvic fracture
- Activate MTP if ABC score >=2 or Shock Index >1.0
- **[KEY STAT: Tourniquet = 15-30 sec; RSI = 3-5 min -- the time gap kills]**

> **Bottom Line:** In exsanguination, stop the bleeding before managing the airway.


**Sources:**
- [5] [Holcomb 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25647203/)
- [11] [Eastridge 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23192066/)
- [18] American 2025

## The Airway Algorithm Moves From Basic Maneuvers to RSI to Surgical Airway

- Jaw thrust + suction + remove debris first
- RSI = Rapid Sequence Intubation (sedative + paralytic)
- GCS <=8: strong indication for definitive airway
- Surgical cricothyrotomy if RSI fails or anatomy precludes intubation
- **[KEY STAT: GCS <=8 is a strong indication, but clinical context determines the decision]**

> **Bottom Line:** Assess, protect, then secure the airway -- always have a surgical backup plan.

> **Shelf exam tip:** RSI drugs = sedative (ketamine or etomidate) + paralytic (succinylcholine or rocuronium). Know them.

## MCQ 1: Clinical Decision Point

**35-year-old male, GSW (gunshot wound) to right chest and abdomen. HR 140, BP 70/40, GCS 7. Active abdominal hemorrhage, gurgling respirations. What is your first intervention?**

- A. Rapid sequence intubation (RSI)
- B. Direct pressure to abdominal wound and activate MTP
- C. Bilateral needle decompression
- D. FAST exam (Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma)

> **Bottom Line:** Think xABCDE -- what kills this patient first?

---

## Answer: B -- Control Hemorrhage First in the Exsanguinating Patient

- **Why B:** Active exsanguination is the immediate lethal threat; X before A
- **Why not A:** GCS <=8 alone does not override active hemorrhage control
- **Why not C:** No clinical signs of tension pneumothorax described
- **Why not D:** FAST delays definitive hemorrhage control in the unstable patient

> **Bottom Line:** Treat first what kills first -- hemorrhage before airway in the exsanguinating patient.


**Sources:**
- [11] [Eastridge 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23192066/)
- [18] American 2025

## Tension Pneumothorax Is a Clinical Diagnosis That Requires Immediate Decompression

- Hypotension + absent breath sounds = decompress now
- Never delay for CXR (chest X-ray) or any imaging
- Needle decompression: 2nd ICS MCL or 5th ICS AAL
- Follow with tube thoracostomy (chest tube)
- **[KEY STAT: Clinical diagnosis -- do NOT wait for imaging]**

> **Bottom Line:** Suspected tension pneumothorax is treated, not imaged.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Tension PTX = clinical diagnosis + immediate needle decompression. One of the most tested emergency procedures.


**Sources:**
- [17] [de 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34538823/)

---

## Five Immediately Life-Threatening Chest Injuries Must Be Found in the B Step

- 1. Tension pneumothorax -- needle decompress immediately
- 2. Open pneumothorax -- 3-sided occlusive dressing
- 3. Massive hemothorax -- chest tube; >1,500 mL or >200 mL/hr = OR
- 4. Flail chest -- positive pressure ventilation, pain control
- 5. Cardiac tamponade -- Beck's triad; pericardiocentesis or thoracotomy
- **[KEY STAT: 5 lethal chest injuries -- all diagnosed clinically in the primary survey]**

> **Bottom Line:** Identify and treat all five before moving to the circulation assessment.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Memorize all five. Beck's triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds) = tamponade.


**Sources:**
- [17] [de 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34538823/)
- [18] American 2025

## MCQ 2: Breathing Assessment

**28-year-old female, stab wound to left chest. HR 125, BP 75/45, SpO2 88%. Absent breath sounds on the left, distended neck veins. What is the most appropriate next step?**

- A. Portable chest X-ray (CXR)
- B. Left-sided needle decompression
- C. Intubation and mechanical ventilation
- D. FAST exam to evaluate for cardiac tamponade

> **Bottom Line:** Which B-step intervention cannot wait?

---

## Answer: B -- Needle Decompression for Suspected Tension Pneumothorax

- **Why B:** Absent breath sounds + hypotension + JVD = tension PTX until proven otherwise
- **Why not A:** Imaging delays treatment of a clinical diagnosis
- **Why not C:** Positive pressure ventilation worsens tension PTX before decompression
- **Why not D:** FAST is C-step; B-step life threats are addressed first

> **Bottom Line:** Tension pneumothorax is diagnosed clinically and treated immediately -- never image first.


**Sources:**
- [17] [de 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34538823/)
- [18] American 2025

## "Blood on the Floor and Four More" Localizes the Five Sources of Hemorrhage

- 5 places to bleed: chest, abdomen, pelvis, long bones, external
- FAST (Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma) for abdominal free fluid
- CXR for hemothorax; pelvic XR for fracture
- External hemorrhage should already be controlled in X step
- **[KEY STAT: 5 hemorrhage sources -- chest, abdomen, pelvis, long bones, external]**

> **Bottom Line:** Identify the bleeding source to direct surgical or interventional control.

## Class III and IV Hemorrhagic Shock Demand Immediate Blood Product Resuscitation

| Parameter | Class I | Class II | Class III | Class IV |
|-----------|---------|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Blood loss** | <750 mL (<15%) | 750-1,500 mL (15-30%) | 1,500-2,000 mL (30-40%) | >2,000 mL (>40%) |
| **Heart rate** | <100 | 100-120 | **120-140** | **>140** |
| **Blood pressure** | Normal | Normal | **Decreased** | **Markedly decreased** |
| **Respiratory rate** | 14-20 | 20-30 | 30-40 | >35 |
| **Urine output** | >30 mL/h | 20-30 mL/h | 5-15 mL/h | Negligible |
| **GCS** | 15 | 14-15 | 12-14 | <12 |
| **Treatment** | Monitor | Possible transfusion | **Blood products** | **MTP activate; emergent OR** |

> **Bottom Line:** Our patient (HR 130, BP 80/50, GCS 8) fits Class III-IV -- activate MTP now.

> **Shelf exam tip:** This table is one of the most tested on the surgery shelf. Know the class boundaries and treatments cold.


**Sources:**
- [18] American 2025

## Shock Index and ABC Score Trigger MTP Activation at the Bedside

- Shock Index (SI) = HR / SBP; normal 0.5-0.7
- SI >1.0 = 4-fold increase in mortality risk
- ABC score: penetrating + SBP <=90 + HR >=120 + FAST positive (1 point each)
- ABC >=2 activates MTP (sensitivity 75%, specificity 86%, AUC 0.87)
- **[KEY STAT: Our patient: SI = 130/80 = 1.6 -- far above threshold]**

> **Bottom Line:** Activate MTP on clinical criteria -- do not wait for a hemoglobin result.

## 1:1:1 Balanced Resuscitation Replaced Crystalloid-First Dogma in Hemorrhagic Shock

- PROPPR trial (2015): 1:1:1 pRBC:plasma:platelets ratio
- 1:1:1 achieved hemostasis in 86% vs 78% at 24h (p=0.006)
- Limit crystalloid (NS, LR) to <1 liter before blood products
- Crystalloid worsens the lethal triad (hypothermia + acidosis + coagulopathy)
- **[KEY STAT: PROPPR: 1:1:1 hemostasis 86% vs 78% at 24h (p=0.006, n=680)]**

> **Bottom Line:** Blood products, not crystalloid, are the resuscitation fluid of choice in hemorrhagic shock.

> **Shelf exam tip:** "2 liters normal saline" is almost always wrong in hemorrhagic shock. Choose blood products.


**Sources:**
- [5] [Holcomb 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25647203/)
- [12] [Cannon 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28225743/)
- [13] [LaGrone 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37697470/)

## TXA Must Be Given Within 3 Hours of Injury -- After That, It Causes Harm

- TXA = tranexamic acid (antifibrinolytic drug)
- CRASH-2 (2010): n=20,211, largest trauma RCT ever conducted
- <1 hour: 32% reduction in hemorrhagic death (RR 0.68)
- >3 hours: 44% INCREASE in death (RR 1.44, 95% CI 1.12-1.84)
- Dose: 1g IV bolus over 10 min, then 1g IV over 8 hours
- **[KEY STAT: TXA >3h post-injury INCREASES mortality (RR 1.44)]**

> **Bottom Line:** Give TXA early or not at all -- the 3-hour window is a hard cutoff.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Know CRASH-2 by name, the 3-hour window, the dose, and that late TXA causes harm. Extremely high yield.


**Sources:**
- [3] [Shakur 2010](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20554319/)
- [4] [Roberts 2011](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21439633/)

## FAST Answers One Question: Is There Free Fluid in the Unstable Patient?

- FAST = Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma
- 4 windows: RUQ (Morrison's pouch), LUQ, suprapubic, subxiphoid
- Sensitivity 74%, specificity 98% for free fluid
- Positive FAST + hypotension = OR (operating room)
- Negative FAST does NOT exclude injury (26% miss rate)
- **[KEY STAT: Sensitivity only 74% -- negative FAST cannot rule out injury]**

> **Bottom Line:** FAST rules in; it does not rule out -- a negative FAST demands further workup.

> **Shelf exam tip:** FAST is a "rule-in" test, not a "rule-out" test. The 74% sensitivity is frequently tested.


**Sources:**
- [1] [Netherton 2019](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31317856/)

## MCQ 3: Circulation Management

**Our 22-year-old MVC patient: FAST is positive (free fluid in Morrison's pouch). BP 70/40 despite 2 units pRBCs (packed red blood cells). GCS dropping to 7. What is the most appropriate next step?**

- A. CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast
- B. Emergent exploratory laparotomy
- C. Diagnostic peritoneal lavage (DPL)
- D. 2 liters normal saline bolus

> **Bottom Line:** FAST positive + hemodynamically unstable = what destination?

---

## Answer: B -- FAST Positive and Hemodynamically Unstable Means Emergent Laparotomy

- **Why B:** Positive FAST + refractory hypotension = OR for hemorrhage control
- **Why not A:** CT requires hemodynamic stability; this patient is in extremis
- **Why not C:** DPL is for equivocal FAST; this FAST is clearly positive
- **Why not D:** Crystalloid worsens the lethal triad in hemorrhagic shock

> **Bottom Line:** Positive FAST plus hemodynamic instability equals the operating room.

## GCS Components Plus Pupil Exam Constitute the Rapid Disability Assessment

- GCS = Glasgow Coma Scale: Eye (1-4) + Verbal (1-5) + Motor (1-6) = 3-15
- GCS <=8 = severe TBI (Traumatic Brain Injury)
- Motor component is the best single predictor of outcome
- Pupils: bilateral reactive = reassuring; unilateral fixed = herniation emergency
- Check blood glucose: hypoglycemia mimics TBI
- **[KEY STAT: Always report GCS by components (E/V/M), not just the total -- motor matters most]**

> **Bottom Line:** Report GCS by components, not just the total -- motor score drives prognosis.

> **Shelf exam tip:** Know each GCS component and its scoring range. Motor score is the single best predictor of outcome.


**Sources:**
- [10] [Hatchimonji 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32382780/)
- [18] American 2025

## Exposure and Hypothermia Prevention Must Begin in the Trauma Bay

- Full exposure: cut all clothing, log roll for posterior exam
- Lethal triad: hypothermia + acidosis + coagulopathy
- Warm OR (>24 C), warm blood products, forced-air blankets
- Hypothermia <35 C independently doubles mortality
- **[KEY STAT: 47.8% mortality when the full lethal triad is present]**

> **Bottom Line:** Undress the patient to find injuries, then actively prevent hypothermia.


**Sources:**
- [12] [Cannon 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28225743/)
- [13] [LaGrone 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37697470/)

## Elderly and TBI Patients Require Modified Shock Assessment

- Elderly (>65): beta-blockers mask tachycardia; SBP <110 may = Class III
- TBI: avoid SBP <90 and SpO2 <90; both independently worsen outcome
- TBI: permissive hypotension is ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED
- Anticoagulated: 4-factor PCC for warfarin; idarucizumab for dabigatran
- **[KEY STAT: Elderly on beta-blockers may have normal HR in Class III shock -- lower your threshold]**

> **Bottom Line:** Standard vital sign thresholds fail in special populations -- adjust your suspicion.

> **Shelf exam tip:** "Permissive hypotension is contraindicated in TBI" is a frequently tested exception.


**Sources:**
- [18] American 2025

## When to Call for Help: Escalation Criteria and Your Role in the Trauma Bay

- Unstable after 2 units pRBC = call attending NOW
- GCS dropping despite resuscitation = neurosurgery stat
- Failed airway after 2 RSI attempts = surgical airway
- Your MS3 role: hold pressure, report vitals, know xABCDE
- Stand at the foot of the bed -- observe, assist, communicate
- **[KEY STAT: Recognizing the problem and communicating it clearly saves lives]**

> **Bottom Line:** You belong in the trauma bay -- learn the framework, stay calm, be useful, speak up.

## MCQ 4: Case Evolution and Disposition

**Our 22-year-old MVC patient underwent emergent laparotomy (splenectomy + pelvic packing). Now in SICU, POD 1. Temp 34.8 C, pH 7.18, INR 2.1, platelets 65K. What is the most immediate concern?**

- A. Wound infection requiring antibiotics
- B. The lethal triad requiring aggressive rewarming and product correction
- C. Thrombocytopenia requiring hematology consultation
- D. Metabolic acidosis requiring sodium bicarbonate infusion

> **Bottom Line:** Recognize the lethal triad in a postoperative trauma patient.

---

## Answer: B -- The Lethal Triad Requires Immediate and Simultaneous Correction

- **Why B:** Hypothermia (34.8 C) + acidosis (pH 7.18) + coagulopathy (INR 2.1) = lethal triad
- **Why not A:** Wound infection on POD 1 is extremely unlikely; this is hypothermia, not fever
- **Why not C:** Thrombocytopenia is part of the coagulopathy; treating it alone misses the syndrome
- **Why not D:** Bicarbonate treats the number, not the cause; restoring perfusion corrects acidosis

> **Bottom Line:** The lethal triad must be recognized and corrected as a syndrome, not as isolated lab values.


**Sources:**
- [12] [Cannon 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28225743/)
- [13] [LaGrone 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37697470/)

## Six Pearls That Will Save Lives on Your Surgery Clerkship

1. **X before A** -- hemorrhage control precedes airway in the exsanguinating patient
2. **TXA within 3 hours** -- after 3 hours it increases mortality (CRASH-2, RR 1.44)
3. **Shock Index >1.0** -- calculate HR/SBP on every trauma patient; 4x mortality risk
4. **Negative FAST is not reassuring** -- sensitivity only 74%; pursue further workup
5. **Prevent the lethal triad** -- warm the patient, warm the blood, limit crystalloid
6. **Show up and speak up** -- your presence and clear communication save lives

> **Bottom Line:** xABCDE saves lives -- treat first what kills first.
